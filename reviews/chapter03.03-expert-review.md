## Overall Assessment

**Verdict: PASS**

This is a strong third recipe for the Anomaly Detection chapter and the densest payment-integrity writing in the cookbook to date. The Thursday-afternoon vignette (a payment integrity analyst at a regional payer staring at 142,000 claims across 18,000 billing providers, the dermatology practice billing 97110 on eighty-four claims when most dermatologists bill it zero times a year, the internist whose codes-per-visit jumped from two to five over two months, the urgent care whose E&M distribution slid from a bell curve at 99213 to a distribution leaning on 99214 and 99215, the team's investigation capacity of forty cases per month against a 1% anomaly rate that produces 180 candidates) lands the operational reality in three paragraphs. The five concrete behavioral-drift examples (CCM expansion, PT four-code regimen, HCPCS G0299 unit-rounding at exactly four units, modifier 59 at 42% of claims, lab CPT-mix shift with 18% per-claim dollar increase) are the kind of dense-and-specific framing that signals a writer who has been in payment integrity work, and each one maps to a real billing scenario any payment integrity director would recognize. The three-flavors-of-anomaly framing (point, contextual, collective) is the right teaching anchor and recurs through the rest of the recipe; the explicit observation that "a billing anomaly detector isn't really a 'score the incoming claim' system. It's a 'continuously model each provider's behavior and flag the changes' system" is the kind of architectural reframe that distinguishes this recipe from generic anomaly-detection writing. The three-comparison-axes section (self vs. peer vs. expected-given-patient-mix) is publication-ready voice on the design decisions most teams get wrong. The feature-families taxonomy is correctly ordered by signal-to-effort ratio with the right closing observation that "Most teams build the self-comparison and peer-comparison features first and get 80% of the value." The statistical-methods progression (z-scores → control charts/CUSUM → Isolation Forest → matrix factorization → gradient boosting → embeddings) is the right pedagogical order, and the recipe correctly says "A reasonable progression: start with z-scores and CUSUM. Add Isolation Forest once you've seen enough data to tune thresholds." The peer-group subsection, the labeling-problem subsection (with the specific call-out that "Most investigations don't close with a clean 'fraud' or 'not fraud' label"), and the fairness/legitimacy/harassment-of-legitimate-variation subsection are all hard-won-experience teaching that a working payment integrity director would write. The four-stage architecture (provider-period aggregator → peer-group assembler → anomaly scoring → case assembly + prioritization, plus feedback loop) is the architecturally correct factoring and the diagram and walkthrough align with each other (no diagram-vs-walkthrough drift, unlike Recipe 3.1). The Honest Take is publication-ready: the seven lessons (feature engineering matters more than the model, peer group definition is the most consequential design decision, the operational cost of false positives is worse than you think, the narrative summary is surprisingly important, the Isolation Forest catches things z-scores don't, the don't-build-supervised-first lesson, the don't-let-the-system-drive-toward-maximize-cases trap) land the right teaching priorities and end with the right operational metric framing ("dollars recovered per analyst hour" or "investigations-closed-with-action per case created," not volume).

Style hygiene is clean. Zero em dashes (direct U+2014 character check: zero matches). Zero en dashes in prose (the 8 detected en dash matches are inside ASCII art box-drawing characters in the architecture diagram, not in narrative text), an even cleaner posture than Recipes 3.1 and 3.2. No marketing language ("leverage," "seamless," "unlock," "transform," "empower," "revolutionize" all absent). No documentation-voice. The 70/30 vendor balance is preserved cleanly: the conceptual sections (Problem, Technology, General Architecture Pattern) are vendor-neutral and a reader on GCP or Azure could substitute their cloud's primitives without rewriting any of the teaching; AWS service names enter at the AWS Implementation section and stay there. HTML-comment TODOs (3 total) are all forward-placeholder for industry-source citation or aws-samples repository verification, which is the chapter-wide settled posture.

The most consequential cluster of findings is around domain accuracy in the E&M feature engineering and the recurring trigger-idempotency pattern. The 2021 CMS/AMA E/M documentation overhaul deleted CPT 99201, restructured documentation requirements for 99202-99215, and produced a structural break in historical E&M distributions that affected nearly every outpatient provider in the United States simultaneously. The 2023 inpatient/observation E&M changes (99221-99239 and the 99217-99220 / 99224-99226 / 99231-99239 reorganization) produced a similar structural break for inpatient codes. The recipe presents the E&M-distribution-shift example ("A provider whose E&M distribution shifts from centered-on-99213 to centered-on-99214") as the canonical upcoding pattern without acknowledging that a population-wide shift in this exact direction occurred legitimately around 2021-01-01 because the documentation rules changed. The recipe's "Where it struggles" section addresses payer-specific policy changes ("When the payer changes a policy ... provider billing adjusts. The adjustments will look like anomalies until the baselines catch up. Pause or dampen the signals during known policy rollouts") but doesn't extend this discipline to industry-wide CMS/AMA coding changes. The pseudocode's E&M level distribution treats levels 1 through 5 as a stable five-bucket categorical (`"level_1": ..., "level_2": ..., ..., "level_5": ...`); for new-patient codes, level 1 (99201) does not exist post-2021 and the bucket should not appear in current-period feature vectors. The Python companion's `EM_CODES` dictionary (per the existing code review at `reviews/chapter03.03-code-review.md` Finding 5) explicitly includes 99201, which carries the bug forward into runnable code.

Several MEDIUM findings cluster on architectural completeness, the recurring trigger-idempotency pattern, and PHI/fairness governance: outcome-event idempotency at the EventBridge → outcome-joiner Lambda path is not modeled (recurring across Recipes 2.4-2.10, 3.1, 3.2 and now 3.3); no DLQ or poison-message handling for the case-assembly, scoring, or outcome-joiner Lambdas; the SHAP-explanation pseudocode call (`top_contributors: shap_explain(isolation_forest, record, top_k = 5)`) misrepresents how SHAP applies to Isolation Forest (TreeSHAP doesn't directly support IF; Kernel SHAP works but is computationally expensive; common production patterns use path-length attribution or feature-mean deviations as a proxy); SNS analyst-notification payload PHI minimization is not explicit (the chapter-3-settled "case-id-only" convention from Recipe 3.1 is implied but not stated); subgroup data governance for fairness monitoring is acknowledged in prose ("Coordinate with provider-network operations on what attributes are available") but the architectural artifacts that make subgroup monitoring binding are not specified.

Priority breakdown: 0 CRITICAL, 1 HIGH, 6 MEDIUM, 6 LOW.

The risk profile is similar to Recipe 3.1 (no LLM in the core path, no clinical-recommendation surface, no FDA medical-device exposure) with the same financial-and-operational stakes (provider relationship damage from false positives, recoverable-dollar leakage from false negatives) rather than patient harm. #### Finding S1: SNS Analyst-Notification Payload PHI Minimization Not Explicitly Specified

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Security
- **Location:** Step 4 `assemble_cases`, the SNS publish at the end (`SNS.Publish(topic = ANALYST_NOTIFICATION_TOPIC, message = { case_id, provider_id, severity, routing, exposure })`); Why-These-Services Amazon SNS paragraph; Prerequisites IAM row.

The risk is low but the inconsistency teaches readers a different convention than Recipes 3.1 and 3.2 did, and SNS subscriber endpoints (especially email/SMS/Slack via downstream integrations) are exactly the surface where lock-screen previews and shared-device exposure produce real PHI leakage.
- **Fix:** Update the Step 4 pseudocode to publish only `case_id` and a coarse routing tier (e.g., "high-severity" / "clinical-review") in the SNS payload, with the comment: "The message carries the case id and routing tier only; the analyst UI fetches the full case record (including provider_id, signals, evidence claims, exposure) by id so the notification channel never carries PHI or PHI-adjacent identifiers." #### Finding S3: Per-Consumer IAM Scoping for Queue-Table Access Not Explicit

#### Finding S4: Investigation Outcome and Decision Audit Trail Not Bound to a Tamper-Evident Store

- **Severity:** LOW
- **Expert:** Security / Compliance
- **Location:** Step 5 `on_investigation_outcome` (writes to `case-registry` and `labels-parquet`); Prerequisites Encryption row; Retention row.
- **Problem:** Investigation outcomes (disposition, resolution notes, dollars recovered, SIU referral status) are the source-of-truth records for any downstream provider grievance, regulatory inquiry, SIU continuation, or law enforcement referral. The recipe writes them to `case-registry` (DynamoDB, mutable per-item) and to `labels-parquet` (S3, mutable unless explicit Object Lock is applied). DynamoDB items are mutable: the table owner can update or delete the item and the change is reversible only via point-in-time recovery within the PITR window (35 days). For a record that may be subpoenaed in a fraud investigation 2-7 years after creation (the multi-regime retention horizon), the case-registry default offering is weaker than the audit posture warrants. Same finding shape as Recipe 3.1 S2 (auto-suspension records).

  Two production patterns are common: (a) write a parallel decision record to S3 with Object Lock in COMPLIANCE mode at the time the outcome is captured (the recipe specifies S3 for `labels-parquet` but does not specify Object Lock), or (b) write decision records to Amazon QLDB for cryptographic verification of decision history. The recipe's CloudTrail data-events discipline addresses the read-trail concern but not the write-immutability concern.
- **Fix:** Add to the Encryption row or to a new Auditability row in Prerequisites: "Investigation outcome records and disposition decisions are subject to provider grievance, regulatory inquiry, and SIU/law-enforcement subpoena. Configure S3 Object Lock in COMPLIANCE mode on the `labels-parquet` bucket in production (GOVERNANCE mode in dev so test data can be cleaned up). Optionally write a parallel decision-record log to Amazon QLDB if cryptographic verification of decision history is required by your organization's compliance posture." Add a one-line mention in "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" under the existing "Data retention is complicated" bullet.

### Architecture Expert Review

#### What's Done Well

- The four-stage pipeline (provider-period aggregator → peer-group assembler → anomaly scoring → case assembly + prioritization, plus a feedback loop through investigation outcomes back to retraining) is the architecturally correct factoring for this problem class. The decision to make the unit of analysis the provider-period (not the individual claim) is the architectural reframe most first-time builders miss, and the recipe is explicit about it ("a billing anomaly detector isn't really a 'score the incoming claim' system. It's a 'continuously model each provider's behavior and flag the changes' system. The unit of analysis is typically the provider-month or provider-week, not the individual claim").
- Three-axis comparison framing (self vs. peer vs. expected-given-patient-mix) is the right factoring, and the recipe correctly identifies that "Most mature systems run all three comparisons in parallel and combine the signals."
- Three signal families (peer z-scores, self-history CUSUM, multivariate Isolation Forest) are kept architecturally separate through the pipeline ("Each signal is kept separately rather than aggregated into a single score prematurely, so the case assembly layer can explain which signal fired"). This is the right pedagogical posture for a system that needs to explain itself to analysts.
- The leave-one-out discipline for peer group statistics is named explicitly with the right operational framing: "if you leave them in, you're comparing them to themselves (and if they're an extreme outlier, they're pulling the group statistic toward themselves). Leave-one-out z-scores fix this. It sounds fiddly. It matters more than you'd think when you have specialties with fewer than 100 providers." This is the kind of detail-level teaching the cookbook is built on.
- Case assembly as a first-class architectural step ("This is the step most first-time builders under-invest in. The raw output of the scoring layer is a pile of signals (tens of thousands per period). The operations team can work tens of cases per analyst per month") is the operationally correct framing. Most generic anomaly-detection writeups skip this and produce systems that fire signals into a queue analysts can't process.
- The peer-group fallback hierarchy (specialty + region + setting → specialty + region → specialty) with the minimum-group-size guard is the right configuration-driven approach, and the recipe correctly says "Hierarchical peer groups work well: run the comparison at multiple levels (specialty, specialty+region, specialty+region+setting) and use whichever has enough comparators."

#### Finding A1: E&M Coding Overhaul Context (2021 CMS/AMA Changes) Not Acknowledged; Pseudocode Treats E&M Levels 1-5 as Stable for New-Patient Codes

- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Expert:** Architecture / Domain Accuracy
- **Location:** Step 1 `rollup_provider_period`, the E&M level distribution computation:
  ```
  features.em_distribution = {
      "level_1": count_where(em_claims, level == 1) / length(em_claims),
      "level_2": count_where(em_claims, level == 2) / length(em_claims),
      "level_3": count_where(em_claims, level == 3) / length(em_claims),
      "level_4": count_where(em_claims, level == 4) / length(em_claims),
      "level_5": count_where(em_claims, level == 5) / length(em_claims)
  }
  features.em_avg_level = mean(em_claims.level)
  ```
  Also "What 'Anomalous' Means" subsection's example: "A provider whose E&M distribution shifts from centered-on-99213 to centered-on-99214 isn't wrong on any single claim. The pattern across thousands of claims is the signal." Also "Where it Struggles" bullet on payer-specific policy changes (which acknowledges the right discipline for payer-specific changes but not for industry-wide CMS/AMA changes).
- **Problem:** Three connected gaps:

  1. **CPT 99201 was deleted from CPT effective January 1, 2021** as part of the CMS/AMA E/M documentation overhaul. The current new-patient office/outpatient codes are 99202-99205 (levels 2-5); level 1 for new patients no longer exists. The pseudocode's `level_1` bucket implicitly treats the new-patient level 1 as a non-empty category and feeds it into z-score and CUSUM computations. For modern claims data (2021 forward) this bucket is dominated by 99211 (established-patient level 1), and any provider with significant new-patient volume produces feature vectors that are sensitive to whether the implementation correctly maps codes to levels. The Python companion's `EM_CODES` dictionary explicitly includes 99201 (per the existing code review's Finding 5); a reader who copies the pseudocode and the Python companion will produce a feature vector with a stale code mapping.

  2. **The 2021 E&M overhaul produced a population-wide distributional shift** in the exact direction the recipe presents as the canonical upcoding pattern. The 2021 documentation rule changes substantially raised the share of legitimate level-4 and level-5 office visits relative to level-3 (because the documentation thresholds for level 4 and level 5 were either reduced or made easier to satisfy via medical-decision-making criteria rather than history-and-physical bullet counts). For a CUSUM detector operating on a 12-month rolling window in early 2021 through mid-2022, every outpatient provider in the United States produced a sustained shift in their E&M distribution. The recipe's "Where it Struggles" section addresses payer-specific policy changes ("provider billing adjusts. The adjustments will look like anomalies until the baselines catch up. Pause or dampen the signals during known policy rollouts") but doesn't extend this discipline to industry-wide coding changes. A reader operating this system in early 2026 is well past the 2021 transition, but a reader doing historical analysis (case mining over 2019-2022 data) or a reader operating during a future industry-wide change (the 2023 inpatient/observation E&M overhaul affecting 99221-99239 was the most recent example) will produce false-positive signals at industry scale.

  3. **The "shift from 99213 to 99214" canonical example needs the temporal caveat.** The recipe's most-quotable upcoding example ("the urgent care in the suburbs where the distribution of evaluation and management levels went from a reasonable-looking bell curve centered at 99213 (level 3) to a distribution that leans almost entirely on 99214 and 99215 (levels 4 and 5)") is correct as a pattern-recognition example but should acknowledge that the same shift, if observed across the entire peer group during 2021-Q1, is legitimate documentation-rule alignment rather than upcoding. The peer-group comparison naturally normalizes this (everyone shifted together, so the relative z-scores stay stable), but the self-CUSUM detector will fire on every provider during the transition window.

  This is HIGH severity because: (a) the pseudocode contains a feature-engineering structure that's stale relative to current CPT (level 1 for new patients no longer exists), (b) the recipe presents a canonical upcoding example without the temporal caveat that affected every U.S. outpatient provider for ~18 months, (c) the peer-baseline-vs-self-baseline distinction the recipe centers on is exactly the discipline that would have failed during the 2021 transition (self-CUSUM fires on everyone, peer comparisons stay stable), and (d) the Python companion carries the stale code mapping forward.
- **Fix:** Three coordinated edits:

  1. **Update the pseudocode's E&M level distribution** to acknowledge the new/established split and the post-2021 reality:
     ```
     // E&M level distribution. Note that CPT 99201 was deleted effective 2021-01-01;
     // current new-patient codes are 99202-99205 (levels 2-5). Track new-patient and
     // established-patient distributions separately, since the documentation rules
     // that govern them differ and structural breaks (e.g., the 2021 E/M overhaul)
     // affect them differently.
     em_new_patient_claims    = em_claims WHERE procedure_code in NEW_PATIENT_EM_CODES
     em_established_claims    = em_claims WHERE procedure_code in ESTABLISHED_EM_CODES
     features.em_new_patient_distribution    = level_distribution(em_new_patient_claims, levels=[2,3,4,5])
     features.em_established_distribution    = level_distribution(em_established_claims, levels=[1,2,3,4,5])
     features.em_avg_level                   = mean(em_claims.level)
     ```

  2. **Add an industry-wide-coding-change discipline to "Where it Struggles."** Insert a new bullet alongside the existing payer-specific policy-change bullet: "Industry-wide coding rule changes. CMS/AMA periodically restructures coding rules across whole code families: the 2021 E/M documentation overhaul (99201 deleted, 99202-99215 documentation rules changed, level shifts industry-wide) and the 2023 inpatient/observation E/M reorganization (99217-99220 deleted, 99221-99239 restructured) are the most recent examples. During and after these transitions, every provider's billing pattern shifts in the same direction simultaneously. Self-history CUSUM signals will fire at industry scale; peer comparisons stay roughly stable. Pause or recalibrate self-history signals during known industry-wide transitions, and treat any 12-18 month window straddling a transition as low-confidence for self-comparison purposes."

  3. **Add the temporal caveat to the canonical upcoding example.** Insert a one-line parenthetical after the "shift from centered-on-99213 to centered-on-99214" example: "(In 2021 a similar shift happened across the entire industry as a consequence of the CMS/AMA E/M documentation overhaul, which is exactly why peer comparisons matter as much as self-comparisons; the upcoding signal is the shift relative to peers, not the shift in absolute terms.)"

#### Finding A2: Outcome-Event Idempotency Not Modeled at the Outcome-Joiner Lambda

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture
- **Location:** Step 5 `on_investigation_outcome` (consumed via EventBridge by the outcome-joiner Lambda); architecture diagram (`O[EventBridge\ninvestigation-events] --> Q[AWS Lambda\noutcome-joiner]`).
- **Problem:** The architecture is event-driven from the analyst tooling's investigation-outcome events to the outcome-joiner Lambda via EventBridge. EventBridge guarantees at-least-once delivery; Lambda asynchronous invocation also retries on failure. If EventBridge redelivers an event (the Lambda's first attempt timed out, the analyst tooling's PutEvents call retried after a transient error, or any of the failure modes documented in the recurring trigger-idempotency findings across Recipes 2.4-2.10, 3.1, and 3.2), the outcome-joiner runs twice on the same outcome. The current pseudocode has no idempotency guard. Each run:

  1. Updates the case-registry record. The DynamoDB `PutItem` is idempotent in shape (status moves from "new" to "closed", the key fields are deterministic), but a redelivered outcome event with a slightly different `resolved_at` timestamp (because the analyst tooling re-emitted the event and used a fresh timestamp) overwrites the resolution timestamp inconsistently.

  2. Writes a fresh training row to `labels-parquet` with a UUID-keyed S3 path. The S3 PutObject is "idempotent" only in the sense that two writes don't conflict, but the resulting two training rows for the same case will be picked up by the next quarterly retrain. This biases the supervised classifier's training distribution toward whichever cases happened to be retried, and degrades subgroup metrics in the same direction.

  3. Emits duplicate `case_closed`, `dollars_recovered`, and `outcome_by_severity` CloudWatch metrics, double-counting the outcome in operational dashboards and skewing the dollar-recovery total that the program is reported on.

  Same recurring trigger-idempotency pattern as Recipes 2.4-2.10, 3.1, and 3.2. The fix template is the same: deterministic event-key derivation, conditional-write enforcement.
- **Fix:** Two-part fix:

  1. Derive a deterministic event key from `case_id` plus the outcome disposition, and use it as a write-once guard in DynamoDB:
     ```
     event_key = event.case_id + "|" + event.disposition
     try:
         DynamoDB.PutItem(
             table = "processed-outcomes",
             item = { event_key: event_key, processed_at: NOW() },
             condition = "attribute_not_exists(event_key)"
         )
     except ConditionalCheckFailedException:
         emit_metric("outcome_event_duplicate_dropped", 1)
         RETURN
     ```
     The `processed-outcomes` table can have a TTL of 90 days. This prevents both the case-registry update and the labels-parquet write from running twice for the same outcome event.

  2. Add a "Trigger idempotency" bullet to "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" that ties the discipline to the recurring chapter pattern, with a forward reference to whatever cookbook-wide idempotency appendix gets created.

  This is now the ninth consecutive recipe with this finding (2.4-2.10, 3.1, 3.2, 3.3). The cookbook editor should treat the appendix-consolidation as the highest-leverage cookbook-wide editorial investment.

#### Finding A3: SHAP Explanation for Isolation Forest Mischaracterized in Pseudocode

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture / Domain Accuracy
- **Location:** Step 3 `score_anomalies`, the Isolation Forest signal block:
  ```
  if_score = isolation_forest.score(feature_vector(record))
  IF if_score <= ISOLATION_FOREST_THRESHOLD:
      signals.append({
          type:            "isolation_forest",
          anomaly_score:   if_score,
          top_contributors: shap_explain(isolation_forest, record, top_k = 5),
          severity:        if_score_to_severity(if_score)
      })
  ```
  Also "Statistical Methods That Fit" prose on Isolation Forest: "Interpretability takes extra work (you have to compute per-feature contributions to the anomaly score), but it's doable."
- **Problem:** The pseudocode's `shap_explain(isolation_forest, record, top_k = 5)` call implies a clean SHAP application that doesn't exist for Isolation Forest. SHAP's `TreeExplainer` is the fast, exact explainer for tree-based models, but it's designed for gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost) and certain random forest variants where the model exposes a leaf-level prediction function. Isolation Forest is structurally different: it's an ensemble of randomly-split trees where the "prediction" is the average path length to isolation, not a leaf-level prediction. TreeSHAP doesn't directly apply.

  Three production-realistic options exist for explaining Isolation Forest predictions:

  1. **Kernel SHAP** (model-agnostic). Works on any prediction function. Computationally expensive (typically 30 seconds to several minutes per prediction at the per-feature contribution level), which is impractical at scale for tens of thousands of provider-period scores per month.

  2. **Path-length attribution.** A custom attribution that computes per-feature contributions to the path length, derived from the original Liu/Ting/Zhou Isolation Forest paper. Implementations exist in research code but are not in the SHAP library directly.

  3. **Feature-deviation proxies.** For each provider-period feature, compute the z-score against the training-set mean/stddev (which the model already implicitly used). Surface the top-k features by absolute z-score as "top contributors." This is what most production payment integrity systems actually do, and it's what the Python companion does (per the existing code review's "What Is Clean" section: "Isolation Forest contribution explanation uses training-time means and stddevs captured in the model metadata, not recomputed on the fly; comment correctly flags this as a SHAP proxy and points at the `shap` library for production-grade attribution"). The Python companion gets this right; the pseudocode misrepresents it.

  This matters because: (a) the recipe is teaching ML concepts to a mixed audience and a non-ML reader who copies the pseudocode and tries to call `shap.TreeExplainer(isolation_forest)` will hit either a runtime error or silently incorrect attributions; (b) the operational team will sometimes ask "why is this provider's IF score so anomalous?" and the answer depends on which attribution method is actually used; (c) the gap between the pseudocode's implied simplicity and the production reality is exactly the kind of teaching-fidelity issue that erodes trust in the recipe's other claims.
- **Fix:** Replace the pseudocode call with a proxy-attribution pattern that matches what the Python companion does, and add prose acknowledging the SHAP-on-IF complexity:
  ```
  // Top contributors. SHAP's TreeExplainer doesn't directly apply to Isolation
  // Forest (the model's prediction is path-length-based, not leaf-level), and
  // KernelSHAP is too slow at this volume. Use feature-deviation proxies:
  // for each feature, compute the z-score against the IF training set's
  // per-feature mean and stddev (captured at training time and stored alongside
  // the model artifact); surface the top-k by absolute z-score.
  top_contributors = explain_iforest_by_deviation(
      record         = record,
      feature_stats  = isolation_forest.training_feature_stats,
      top_k          = 5
  )
  ```
  Update the "Statistical Methods That Fit" prose paragraph on Isolation Forest interpretability: "Interpretability takes extra work. SHAP's tree-explainer doesn't directly apply (Isolation Forest's prediction function is path-length-based, not leaf-level); the practical patterns are KernelSHAP (model-agnostic, slow), path-length attribution from the original IF paper (custom implementation), or feature-deviation proxies (z-score against training-set per-feature stats, fast and good enough for analyst-facing explanations). Most production systems use the third option."

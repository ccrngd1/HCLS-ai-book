## Overall Assessment

**Verdict: PASS**

This is the third recipe in the chapter that lands at the "Complex" complexity tier with full production governance scope, and the writing meets the moment. The opening vignette (the cardiology step-down nurse opening the chart of a patient she shares a last name with at 11:47 p.m. on a Tuesday, six audit events out of forty million that night, the chart access that nothing flags because the nurse has the role permissions and the workstation matches and the patient has no VIP flag, the three-week-later complaint from the patient about his cousin asking about a medication he hadn't told her about, the privacy-office investigation, the termination, the OCR breach notification, the civil suit, the local newspaper, and the CIO call from the CEO that begins with "explain to me how this happened") is the densest healthcare-access-monitoring framing in any cookbook recipe and is the closest the cookbook has come to the operational reality of insider snooping. The closing reframe ("Not the Hollywood scenario where a foreign state actor exfiltrates ten million records ... The everyday reality is a workforce of thirty or forty thousand authenticated users, each of whom has been granted broad access to the EHR by clinical necessity, browsing records they shouldn't be looking at for reasons that range from idle curiosity to malicious snooping to credential compromise") is the system reframe most first-time builders miss because they conflate insider snooping with external attack patterns, and the recipe states the distinction plainly.

The "Healthcare has a specific structural problem that makes this hard" framing is the recipe's biggest pedagogical contribution and is the lesson most published treatments of UEBA blur. The framing that broad clinical access is not a bug but a feature (clinicians need broad access because patients move between units, get cross-covered, end up in the ED at 3 a.m., and require care that crosses departmental boundaries; pulling permissions tighter solves the privacy problem and creates the patient-safety problem; patient safety wins every time) is exactly right and is the design lesson that distinguishes mature healthcare access programs from generic UEBA deployments. The "the access is technically authorized; it's just not appropriate" framing is the operationally-correct framing for the entire detection problem.

The eight-paragraph "the reason this problem lands at the complex end of the chapter" subsection (the base rate is brutal; legitimate access patterns are extraordinarily diverse; the treatment relationship is poorly captured in source data; the data is high-dimensional and multi-modal; adversarial dynamics matter; the output isn't an alert, it's an investigation; workforce monitoring has its own legal and labor considerations; the breach notification clock is real; HIPAA Security Rule audit controls are mandatory but underspecified) is the densest treatment of healthcare-access-monitoring complexity anywhere in the cookbook. The "the privacy office staff is small (often single digits across an entire health system) and the reviews are time-consuming (you have to read the access events in context, check the user's role and assignments, often interview the user)" framing is operationally accurate and lands the alert-fatigue math in the same shape as Recipes 3.6, 3.7, and 3.8 with the privacy-office-specific amplification.

The Vocabulary You Need subsection (UEBA, PPM, EHR audit logs, HIPAA Security Rule audit controls, treatment relationship, break-glass, appropriate use review, workforce member) is unusually substantive on the healthcare-specific terminology that distinguishes patient privacy monitoring from general UEBA. Naming the commercial PPM vendors explicitly (Protenus, Imprivata FairWarning, MaizeAnalytics now part of Imprivata, Iatric Patient Privacy Monitor, Epic's Provider Access Audit) and the EHR-specific audit-log mechanisms (Epic Audit Log API, Cerner Behavior Tracker, MEDITECH audit reports) gives the reader the integration landscape rather than abstract framing. The "workforce member" framing for the HIPAA-defined population (employees, contractors, students, volunteers, residents, business associates) is the right scope-defining move.

The Detection Pattern Catalog subsection (same-name access, same-address or same-neighborhood access, self-access and dependent-access, co-worker access, VIP/sensitive-patient access, off-hours and off-shift access, geographic and device anomalies, volume anomalies, search anomalies, print and export anomalies, bulk and reporting anomalies, break-glass override patterns, sequence and workflow anomalies, access to deceased patient records, patterns around news cycles, account abandonment and reactivation, new-user behavior) is the most comprehensive enumeration of healthcare access-pattern detection categories in any cookbook recipe. The per-pattern operational caveats are specific: the same-name pattern weighted by name uniqueness, the same-address pattern requiring HR address linkage, the self-access pattern's policy-dependence, the co-worker access pattern's identity-data sensitivity, the VIP pattern's break-glass interaction, the off-hours pattern's shift-data dependency, the volume anomaly's behavioral-baseline requirement. Each pattern is named with both its detection signature and its operational gotcha.

The Statistical and ML Methods subsection (rules engines, per-user behavioral baselines, peer-group baselines, Isolation Forest and other unsupervised outlier detectors, sequence models, graph-based detection, graph neural networks, autoencoders on access vectors, supervised classification on labeled cases, LLM-assisted triage, feedback-driven threshold tuning) is a layered architecture posture done right. The closing "reasonable layered architecture" recommendation (rules engine for the policy-defined patterns, per-user and peer-group baselines for the deviation patterns, graph features for the relationship patterns, sequence models for the workflow patterns, LLM-assisted triage on top, supervised classifier as program matures) is operations-engineer voice on the most consequential program-design decision in this domain. The Calibration, Subgroup Performance, and the Workforce-Equity Question subsection is unusually substantive on the workforce-equity dimension that distinguishes access monitoring from clinical anomaly detection. The "the workforce members being monitored are often the same employees the organization is trying to retain and engage" framing and the four-pattern treatment (subgroup performance, threshold calibration, workforce communication and transparency, union considerations, appeals and remediation) is the operationally-honest framing that distinguishes mature programs from ones that surveil-without-process. #### Finding S1: Case Payload PHI and Workforce-PII Minimization for Privacy Office UI Back End and SIEM Connector Not Stated in Pseudocode

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Security
- **Location:** Step 7 `build_case` (the `case` dict construction with `evidence`, `narrative`, `shap_top_drivers`, `workforce_record`, `patient_record`, `care_relationship`, `user_recent_activity`); Step 8 `on_investigator_action` (case references); Architecture Diagram (`AD[Amazon EventBridge | case-bus] --> AE[Privacy Office Case Queue UI | AppSync/API Gateway]`, `AD --> AF[SIEM connector | Splunk / Sentinel]`, `AD --> AG[(OpenSearch | case-index)]`); Sample case package in Expected Results.

- **Problem:** Step 7's `build_case` constructs each `case` carrying `case_id`, `workforce_id`, `patient_id`, `tier`, `composite_score`, the full `evidence` package (which includes the `workforce_record` with role, department, manager, employment_type, hire_date, recent_role_changes; the `patient_record` with sensitivity_flags, recent_encounters, care_team_history; the `care_relationship` evidence; the `user_recent_activity` with last_30_days_summary, sensitive_accesses_30d, prior_cases; the `rules_flags` with explanation strings; the `baseline_evidence` with deviation_score and top_z_features; the `graph_evidence` with relationship_evidence; the `sequence_evidence`; the `composite_score`; the `tier`; the `model_version`; and the `shap_top_drivers`). The case is published via EventBridge to three subscribers: the Privacy Office Case Queue UI back end (AppSync/API Gateway), the SIEM connector (Splunk/Sentinel/Chronicle/QRadar), and the OpenSearch case-index.

  The sample case package in Expected Results confirms the shape. The narrative ("Workforce member WF-12849 (registered nurse, cardiology step-down unit) accessed the chart of patient PT-3382091 at 23:47 on May 13, outside the user's documented shift schedule (7 a.m. to 7 p.m. day shift). The user and patient share an uncommon last name (Wojnarowski, name uniqueness 0.81) and the same home ZIP (14620, a small ZIP with approximately 9,400 residents). No care team membership, on-call coverage, encounter assignment, or order signature linking the user to this patient was found. The patient was admitted earlier the same day to a unit other than the user's. The session lasted approximately 90 seconds and included views of demographics, the medication list, and the most recent discharge summary, with no documentation, order entry, or clinical action performed. Recommended for privacy office same-day review.") is explicit content that names both the workforce member's identity, role, department, schedule, and home-ZIP and the patient's identity, sensitivity context, and clinical-evaluation-shaped suggested next steps.

  The chapter-3-settled discipline (Recipes 3.1, 3.3, 3.4, 3.5, 3.6, 3.7, 3.8) is that high-fanout notification channels carry the alert ID only, and the consuming application fetches the full record by ID through an authenticated path so the notification channel never carries PHI. Recipe 3.9's case event-bus pattern is structurally similar: three subscribers (UI, SIEM, OpenSearch) each fetching from EventBridge. A reader who treats the pseudocode as the implementation specification will produce a system where the full case payload (including the narrative and the workforce-and-patient identity context) flows through the EventBridge bus and into each subscriber's downstream stores and logs.

  The recipe-3.9 amplification is that the case payload contains both PHI (patient identifier, patient demographics, sensitivity flags, encounter history) and workforce PII (workforce member identifier, role, department, manager, schedule, home ZIP, hire date, employment type) in equal measure. The recipe correctly identifies that workforce PII must be protected with the same discipline as PHI, but the case-bus payload pattern doesn't enforce minimization for either. SIEM systems specifically have substantially broader access scopes than the privacy-office UI; routing the full case payload (with workforce member identity, role, department, home ZIP, and the patient's encounter history) into the SIEM exposes the data to security operations staff who do not have a privacy-office investigation reason.

  The labor-and-legal dimension is unique to this recipe in the chapter. A workforce member whose case is dismissed-as-legitimate may have had their identity, role, department, manager, schedule, and home ZIP exposed to SIEM operations staff during the routine fanout. Without minimization, this exposure happens for every case (the majority of which are dismissed). For unionized environments and for jurisdictions with employee privacy statutes, this is the architectural discipline that makes the difference between "monitoring with appropriate process" and "monitoring with broader-than-necessary disclosure."

- **Fix:** Update Step 7 pseudocode to make the payload-minimization discipline explicit. The EventBridge case-bus carries only `case_id`, `workforce_id`, `patient_id`, `tier`, and `composite_score`; the consuming back ends (UI back end, SIEM connector, OpenSearch case-index) fetch the full case (with narrative, workforce_record, patient_record, care_relationship, user_recent_activity, evidence, shap_top_drivers) by `case_id` through an authenticated path with appropriate IAM scope on the case-state DynamoDB table and the OpenSearch case-index. PHI and workforce PII do not transit through the EventBridge bus or any logs it generates.

  Add a one-line note: "For the case event bus, the per-case event carries only `case_id`, `workforce_id`, `patient_id`, `tier`, and `composite_score`; subscribers fetch the full case (with narrative, workforce_record, patient_record, care_relationship, user_recent_activity, evidence, and SHAP drivers) from the case-state DynamoDB table or the OpenSearch case-index through an authenticated path scoped to the consumer's role. The narrative is sensitive content (workforce member identity and role context, patient identity and clinical context, suggested clinical-evaluation steps) and must not transit channels with broader access than the privacy-office workflow itself."

  Update the sample case package in Expected Results to show the minimal event-bus payload separately from the full case stored in the case-state DynamoDB table and the OpenSearch case-index.

  Add to Prerequisites IAM row a per-consumer scope: SIEM connector role can read case payload by `case_id` for cases whose `case_class` includes `credential_compromise` or `lateral_movement` only (and does not include curiosity-snooping cases that should remain in the privacy-office workflow); UI back end role can read case payload for the privacy office's investigator team only; OpenSearch case-index role is restricted to privacy-office queries.

  This finding shape is now eight cookbook recipes deep (Chapter 2 prompts; Recipes 3.1, 3.3, 3.4, 3.5, 3.6, 3.7, 3.8) and the consolidation discipline is the same; the appendix-level treatment is the highest-leverage cookbook-wide editorial investment alongside trigger-idempotency.

#### Finding A1: Outcome-Event and Case-Grouping Idempotency Not Modeled at the Capture Lambdas

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture
- **Location:** Step 7 `build_case` (case-grouping path with `find_existing_case` and `update_existing_case`); Step 8 `on_investigator_action` (consumed via EventBridge from the privacy-office UI back end); Architecture Diagram (`AH[Investigator adjudication] --> AI[AWS Lambda outcome-capture]`).

- **Problem:** The architecture is event-driven from the score events stream into `build_case` (publishing to the case-bus), and from the privacy-office UI back end into `on_investigator_action` (consuming from the case-bus). EventBridge guarantees at-least-once delivery; Lambda asynchronous invocation also retries on failure. If EventBridge redelivers a score event, `build_case` runs twice on the same score:

  1. **Searches for existing case** by `(workforce_id, patient_id, within_window_days)`. If the first run created a case (`existing_case is null` first time, then `case = ...`), the second run sees the case and routes to `update_existing_case`. But if the first run is mid-flight at the time of the redelivery, both runs see no existing case and create two duplicate cases for the same `(workforce_id, patient_id)` pair.

  2. **Writes to case-state DynamoDB and OpenSearch case-index**. Two case rows for the same scored event get picked up by the privacy-office case queue.

  3. **Publishes a `CaseOpened` event to the case-bus**. Two events, two downstream subscriber invocations (UI back end, SIEM connector, OpenSearch case-index).

  Same applies to `on_investigator_action`: redelivered outcome events double-update the case state (status transitions twice, outcome_at gets overwritten with the second event's timestamp), double-invoke `initiate_breach_review` (the HIPAA breach-notification clock can be initiated twice with potentially-different start times), double-invoke `refer_to_hr` (HR receives two referral packages for the same case), double-add the suppression rule (which is idempotent in itself but produces duplicate audit-trail entries), and double-write the training label to S3 (which biases the supervised classifier's training distribution toward redelivered cases on a rare positive class).

  Same recurring trigger-idempotency pattern as Recipes 2.4-2.10 and 3.1-3.8 (fourteenth consecutive recipe). The fix template is the same.

  For Recipe 3.9 specifically the consequence is calibrated to three pathways: (1) the supervised retraining pathway (doubled label rows on positive cases distort class balance for the supervised re-ranker, and confirmed-violation cases are the rarest positive class in any chapter-3 recipe so the relative impact is largest); (2) the case-management pathway (duplicate cases for the same `(workforce_id, patient_id)` pair produce fragmented investigations, inflate the case queue, and break the case-grouping intent the recipe explicitly relies on); (3) the HIPAA breach-notification pathway (a redelivered confirmed-violation outcome event can initiate the breach-review clock twice with potentially-different start times; the start time is operationally important because the HIPAA 60-day notification window is anchored to the discovery time, and a doubled clock initiation produces audit-trail ambiguity that complicates the regulatory defense).

  The code review (Finding 1) has already identified the related correctness gap in `find_existing_case` (the `Limit=10 + FilterExpression` scan silently misses an existing open case when the matching item isn't in the first ten unordered records), which produces the case-fragmentation pattern at the Python-companion level. The expert-review finding here is the broader architectural pattern: even if `find_existing_case` is fixed, the at-least-once delivery semantics of EventBridge produce the same fragmentation and double-trigger pattern unless the orchestration layer enforces idempotency.

  With this finding now flagged across fourteen consecutive recipes, the per-recipe-edit posture is producing diminishing returns. The cookbook editor should treat the trigger-idempotency appendix as the highest-leverage cookbook-wide editorial investment.

- **Fix:** Two-part fix:
  1. Derive a deterministic event key from each event's identifier and use it as a write-once guard in DynamoDB before the case-state write, the OpenSearch index, the EventBridge publish, and the breach-review/HR-referral side effects:
     ```
     # Score event idempotency in build_case.
     event_key = score_record.score_id
     try:
         DynamoDB.PutItem(
             table = "processed-score-events",
             item  = { event_key: event_key, processed_at: NOW() },
             condition = "attribute_not_exists(event_key)"
         )
     except ConditionalCheckFailedException:
         emit_metric("score_event_duplicate_dropped", 1)
         RETURN

     # Investigator-action idempotency in on_investigator_action.
     action_key = action.action_id    # provided by the privacy-office UI back end
     try:
         DynamoDB.PutItem(
             table = "processed-investigator-actions",
             item  = { action_key: action_key, processed_at: NOW() },
             condition = "attribute_not_exists(action_key)"
         )
     except ConditionalCheckFailedException:
         emit_metric("investigator_action_duplicate_dropped", 1)
         RETURN
     ```
     The `processed-score-events` and `processed-investigator-actions` tables can have a TTL of 90 days. Apply the pattern to all event-driven side effects.

  2. Add a "Trigger idempotency" bullet to "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" tying the discipline to the recurring chapter pattern. Strongly recommend a cookbook-wide trigger-idempotency appendix.

#### Finding A2: No DLQ or Poison-Message Handling for the Ingest, Normalize, Enrich, Detector, Score, Case-Builder, or Outcome-Capture Lambdas

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture
- **Location:** Architecture Diagram (`B[AWS Lambda ehr-audit-ingest]`, `D[AWS Lambda app-audit-ingest]`, `F[AWS Lambda identity-log-ingest]`, `H[AWS Lambda break-glass-ingest]`, `J[AWS Lambda identity-context-loader]`, `L[AWS Lambda event-normalizer]`, `M[AWS Lambda enrichment]`, `S[AWS Lambda rules-engine]`, `T[AWS Lambda baseline-detector]`, `U[AWS Lambda graph-detector]`, `X[AWS Lambda composite-scoring]`, `Y[AWS Lambda calibration]`, `Z[AWS Lambda case-builder]`, `AI[AWS Lambda outcome-capture]` all without `OnFailure` destinations); Prerequisites table.

- **Problem:** No Dead Letter Queue or `OnFailure` destination is configured for any Lambda in the pipeline. Specific failure modes that surface here:

  - **EHR-audit-ingest Lambda fails on a malformed EHR audit-log payload.** Epic releases a new Audit Log API version that adds a previously-unspecified field, the workforce-identifier resolution fails because the IAM-to-EHR-user-ID mapping cache is stale, the patient-identifier resolution fails because a patient is in the EMPI's "needs-merge" state. Lambda's default async retry is two retries over six hours and then drop. The result is silently lost: no canonical event reaches the enrichment layer, no detector scores the event, the case never enters the case queue. For a system whose entire value proposition is detecting access patterns the workforce member doesn't know is being tracked, silently-failing ingestion is the silent-failure mode that most undermines the program.

  - **Event-normalizer Lambda fails during identifier resolution or canonical-event construction** (DynamoDB throttle on workforce-identity during a thundering-herd event, identifier-resolution timeout on the EMPI lookup, schema-validation failure on a vendor's unexpected payload). Same retry-then-drop pattern. The canonical event never reaches the enrichment layer.

  - **Enrichment Lambda fails during identity/schedule/care-team/sensitivity enrichment** (DynamoDB throttle on workforce-identity, Neptune query timeout on care-relationship lookup, sensitivity-flag fetch failure). The event is not enriched. Detectors that need enrichment data (rules engine, baseline detector, graph detector) score the event with missing context, producing false negatives or wrong scores.

## Overall Assessment

**Verdict: PASS**

This is the first recipe in the cookbook that lands at the "Complex" complexity tier in the chapter, and the writing meets the moment. The 3:14 a.m. medical-surgical-floor sepsis vignette (32-bed unit, two charge nurses sharing a CNA, 67-year-old post-op day 2 from colon resection, vitals charted at 11 p.m. as "mostly normal" by single-threshold rules, daughter pressing the call button at 1:40 a.m. with the patient confused, recollect K stayed at 4.2 from baseline because this is sepsis not pseudohyperkalemia, lactate 4.2, broad-spectrum antibiotics at 2:38 a.m., ICU at 4:15 a.m., nine extra days of stay and three weeks of inpatient rehab, and the data trail of HR climbing from 78 to 92 over eight hours, RR creeping from 14 to 18, temperature rising from 37.1 to 37.8, white count of 14.2, creatinine 0.3 above baseline, "patient appears slightly more somnolent than earlier shift, easily arousable" in the 9 p.m. nursing note) lands the operational reality of subclinical deterioration in three paragraphs. The closing question ("which patients on the floor right now are showing the early signs of deterioration, hours before they hit any single alarm threshold, so the team can intervene before the call button gets pressed at 1:40 a.m.") is the reframe most first-time builders miss and the recipe states it plainly.

The track-and-trigger lineage subsection is clinically accurate and pedagogically correct: MEWS as the original additive scoring system, NEWS and NEWS2 with the Royal College of Physicians provenance and the 2017 NEWS2 revision adding separate handling for chronic hypoxic respiratory disease, qSOFA as sepsis-specific, PEWS for pediatrics with parental concern as a formal criterion, SIRS as the older non-specific antecedent that hospital protocols still reference. The "score of 4 from two parameters slightly off is treated the same as a 4 from one parameter way off" framing is the kind of detail-level teaching that a senior hospitalist or rapid-response nurse would recognize. The "what ML adds (and where it adds less than vendors suggest)" subsection is the right pedagogical reframe: patient-specific baselines, trajectory awareness, multivariate fusion are real ML wins; replacing the underlying clinical reasoning or operational integration is not. The honest framing that "the vendor literature talks a lot about model performance and not enough about the workflow problem; the published peer-reviewed literature is more honest about it" sets up the rest of the recipe well.

The "two big vendor models, and why you should know their reputations" subsection is unusually candid. Naming Epic Deterioration Index and eCART by name, naming the 2021 Wong et al. external validation study and its finding that vendor-reported performance often doesn't match local-population performance, and the explicit "the right answer for many hospitals is to deploy a commercial model with strong local validation rather than build from scratch" framing is the design lesson most first-time builders miss. This subsection is the cookbook's first published treatment of vendor-versus-build for clinical ML, and the framing is correct. The HTML-comment TODO acknowledging the need to confirm the Wong et al. JAMA Internal Medicine citation and recent EDI external validation results is the right discipline.

The statistical and ML methods subsection is the broadest method survey in any chapter-3 recipe (logistic regression with engineered features, gradient-boosted trees, recurrent neural networks, transformer-based time series models, survival analysis, multi-task learning, phenotype-specific models, ensemble combinations). The "reasonable progression" closing posture (start with NEWS2 baseline, then engineered LR and GBT, then iterate, then add LSTM or transformer only if the marginal gain justifies the operational complexity) is the kind of operations-engineer voice the cookbook is built on. The outcome-definition subsection is unusually substantive: ICU transfer with the policy-variation caveat, cardiac arrest and code blue with the lower-base-rate caveat, composite endpoints with the phenotype-mixing caveat, mortality with the lagging-indicator caveat, phenotype-specific outcomes with the labeling-challenges caveat, time-windowed prediction with the operationally-useful framing. The sampling-time-windows-and-right-censoring subsection correctly names time-grid snapping, prediction window construction with the patient-level train/test split, right censoring with the survival-modeling alternative, event leakage with the 30-60 minute lookahead boundary, and treatment leakage with the antibiotics-versus-deterioration confounding. #### Finding S3: Per-Consumer IAM Scoping for Patient State, Alert State, and Scoring History Not Explicit

- **Severity:** LOW
- **Expert:** Security
- **Location:** Step 4 `compute_features` (DynamoDB.GetItem on patient-state); Step 5 `score_patient` (DynamoDB.PutItem to scoring-history); Step 7 `route_alert` (DynamoDB.PutItem to alert-state, OpenSearch.Index to alert-index); Step 8 acknowledgment and outcome capture (DynamoDB updates to alert-state, OpenSearch updates); Prerequisites IAM row.
- **Problem:** The patient-state DynamoDB table, the scoring-history DynamoDB table, the alert-state DynamoDB table, and the alert-index OpenSearch domain are read by multiple Lambdas and clinician roles. The Prerequisites IAM row gives generic least-privilege framing but does not break out per-consumer roles for these shared resources.

  For a system that informs bedside clinical decision-making, blast-radius minimization matters: a compromised role with broad alert-state-write access could silently corrupt alert state (mark an alert as acknowledged when it wasn't, link an outcome to the wrong alert, escalate a low-tier alert into the page channel), which would propagate into wrong clinical decisions and wrong outcome-capture for retraining. A compromised role with broad patient-state-read access exposes the rolling history of every admitted patient, not just the patients on the role's assigned units.
- **Fix:** Strengthen the Prerequisites IAM row with per-consumer scoping: "Event-normalizer Lambda role: `dynamodb:UpdateItem` on patient-state only (no read of other patients' state); `kinesis:GetRecords` on the EHR event stream; `timestream:WriteRecords` on the deterioration-history database. Scoring-orchestrator Lambda role: `dynamodb:GetItem` on patient-state, `timestream:Select` on deterioration-history, `sagemaker:InvokeEndpoint` on the deterioration-model endpoint, `events:PutEvents` to the deterioration-scoring bus only. Alert-router Lambda role: consumes from deterioration-scoring bus; `dynamodb:UpdateItem` on alert-state; OpenSearch index permissions on the alert-index only; `sns:Publish` on the pager-integration topic only; no read of patient state outside the alert being routed. Acknowledgment-capture Lambda role: `dynamodb:UpdateItem` on alert-state by alert_id only; OpenSearch update permission on alert-index; no read of other alerts. Outcome-capture Lambda role: read on alert-index by patient and time range; `s3:PutObject` on training-labels bucket only. Bedside nurse roles: read alert-state by alert_id scoped by case assignment; write acknowledgment-event to the acknowledgment bus only. Charge nurse roles: read alert-state for alerts on their assigned units only. Clinical governance committee roles: read aggregated subgroup-metrics view only; row-level read on alert audit index requires explicit elevation with documented purpose."

#### Finding S4: HL7 v2 / FHIR R4 EHR Ingress Security Posture Not Specified

- **Severity:** LOW
- **Expert:** Security / Networking
- **Location:** Why-These-Services "Amazon Kinesis Data Streams (or Amazon MSK) for the EHR event feed" paragraph; Architecture Diagram (`A[EHR vitals feed | HL7/FHIR] --> B[Amazon Kinesis | clinical-events]`); Prerequisites EHR Integration row ("Real-time ingest typically requires HL7 v2 ADT, ORU (results), ORM (orders), or FHIR R4 subscriptions").

#### Finding A1: Outcome-Event Idempotency Not Modeled at the Outcome-Capture Lambda

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture
- **Location:** Step 8 `on_clinical_outcome` (consumed via EventBridge from clinical-event publishers and from the EHR-events stream); Architecture Diagram (`AE[Outcome events | ICU transfer / Code / Sepsis bundle] --> AF[AWS Lambda | outcome-capture]`); Step 8 `on_clinician_acknowledgment` (similar event-driven path).
- **Problem:** The architecture is event-driven from the EHR for outcome events (ICU transfers, code blues, sepsis bundle initiations, unexpected deaths, discharges) and from the clinician-facing UI for acknowledgment events. EventBridge guarantees at-least-once delivery; Lambda asynchronous invocation also retries on failure. If EventBridge redelivers an outcome event (the Lambda's first attempt timed out, the EHR-events publisher's PutEvents call retried after a transient error, or any of the failure modes documented in the recurring trigger-idempotency findings across Recipes 2.4-2.10 and 3.1-3.6), `on_clinical_outcome` runs twice on the same outcome. Each run:

  1. **Searches OpenSearch for recent alerts on the patient and links the outcome to each.** The OpenSearch search is idempotent in shape, but the linkage update writes `alert.outcome` on each matching alert with a `time_from_alert_to_outcome_minutes` value derived from `outlier_event_id` and `outcome_event.occurred_at`. A redelivered event with a slightly different timestamp overwrites the linkage timestamp inconsistently; downstream metrics (mean time-from-alert-to-outcome, alert-precision-by-outcome-type) get noisy.

  2. **Writes a fresh label row to S3 with a UUID-keyed path** (`S3.PutObject(... key = f"outcomes/year={...}/month={...}/{generate_id()}.json", ...)`). Two label rows for the same outcome get picked up by the next quarterly retraining job. This biases the supervised classifier's training distribution toward whichever cases happened to be redelivered.

  3. **Re-fetches recent alerts** (the `OpenSearch.Search` call). This is the expensive operation; redelivery doubles the cost without producing additional value.

  Same recurring trigger-idempotency pattern as Recipes 2.4-2.10, 3.1, 3.2, 3.3, 3.4, 3.5, 3.6 (twelfth consecutive recipe). The fix template is the same.

  For Recipe 3.7 specifically the consequence is calibrated to the supervised-retraining pathway: doubled label rows on positive cases distort the supervised classifier's class-balance and produce a model that over-fires on patterns that happen to have been redelivered during the training window. For a deterioration model where the positive class is rare (ICU transfers happen at most a few percent of admissions) and labels are precious, doubled labels on a minority of cases are a meaningful training-distribution artifact that propagates into production scoring. With this finding now flagged across twelve consecutive recipes, the cookbook editor should treat the trigger-idempotency appendix as the highest-leverage cookbook-wide editorial investment. The per-recipe editorial loop is producing diminishing returns.
- **Fix:** Two-part fix:
  1. Derive a deterministic event key from the outcome-event identifier (`outcome_event.event_id` from the EHR event publisher) plus the outcome-event type, and use it as a write-once guard in DynamoDB before the OpenSearch search and the S3 label write:
     ```
     event_key = outcome_event.event_id + "|" + outcome_event.type
     try:
         DynamoDB.PutItem(
             table = "processed-outcome-events",
             item  = { event_key: event_key, processed_at: NOW() },
             condition = "attribute_not_exists(event_key)"
         )
     except ConditionalCheckFailedException:
         emit_metric("outcome_event_duplicate_dropped", 1)
         RETURN
     ```
     The processed-outcome-events table can have a TTL of 90 days. This prevents the OpenSearch search, the linkage write, and the S3 label write from running twice for the same outcome event. Apply the same pattern to `on_clinician_acknowledgment` keyed on `acknowledgment_event_id`.
  2. Add a "Trigger idempotency" bullet to "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" tying the discipline to the recurring chapter pattern. Strongly recommend a cookbook-wide trigger-idempotency appendix.

#### Finding A2: No DLQ or Poison-Message Handling for Event-Normalizer, Scoring-Orchestrator, Alert-Router, Acknowledgment-Capture, or Outcome-Capture Lambdas

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture
- **Location:** Architecture Diagram (`H[AWS Lambda | event-normalizer]`, `L[AWS Lambda | scoring-orchestrator]`, `V[AWS Lambda | alert-router]`, `AD[AWS Lambda | ack-capture]`, `AF[AWS Lambda | outcome-capture]` all without `OnFailure` destinations); Prerequisites table.
- **Problem:** No Dead Letter Queue or `OnFailure` destination is configured for any Lambda in the pipeline. The recipe's "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" section names "Disaster recovery and continuity" and "The fallback must be documented: when the system is down, the existing track-and-trigger system (NEWS2 charted in the flowsheet) is the backstop, and the staff need to know that," but the per-event poison-message recovery discipline is at a different abstraction level than that paragraph addresses.

  Specific failure modes that surface here:

  - **Event-normalizer Lambda fails on a malformed message.** The EHR emits an unexpected HL7 segment (rare but happens with new EHR-vendor-specific extensions), the LOINC mapping fails because the lookup table is stale, the unit-conversion encounters an unhandled unit string, the patient master-index resolution times out. Lambda's default async retry behavior is two retries over six hours and then drop. The result is silently lost: no canonical event reaches the patient state store, no scoring fires for that patient, the rolling history is missing the data point. For a deterioration system whose value proposition depends on continuous patient state coverage, silently-failing ingestion is the worst possible failure mode.

  - **Scoring-orchestrator Lambda fails during feature computation or scoring** (DynamoDB throttle on patient-state, Timestream timeout on trajectory query, SageMaker endpoint cold-start latency, Bedrock throttling on explanation generation). Same retry-then-drop pattern. The patient is not scored on the affected event. For an event-driven score path that fires on high-importance state changes (a new lactate, a new respiratory rate), a missed scoring is a missed alert opportunity that may never be caught up by the periodic-tick path within the deterioration window.

  - **Alert-router Lambda fails on a malformed score event** (the calibration layer emits an unexpected schema, the explanation layer's Bedrock call fails with the alert reaching the router without a narrative, the patient state being read by the router is inconsistent because of a concurrent state update). The alert is never published; the pager doesn't fire; the dashboard doesn't update; the EHR banner doesn't show.

  - **Acknowledgment-capture Lambda fails on a malformed acknowledgment event** (the clinician UI emits an unexpected disposition value, the alert_id references an alert that has aged out of the alert-state retention window, the disposition's intervention free-text exceeds the DynamoDB attribute size limit). The acknowledgment is never recorded; the alert stays "pending" forever; the time-to-acknowledge metric is never computed for that alert.

  - **Outcome-capture Lambda fails on a malformed outcome event** (the EHR emits an unexpected outcome type code, the patient_id references a patient outside the scope of the deployment, the linkage fails because of a Time-since-alert-to-outcome calculation error). The label is never written; the case never enters the labeled-outcomes archive; the supervised classifier's next retraining run misses the data point.

  Without DLQs, these failures appear only in CloudWatch Logs and require log-trawling to discover. For a system in the alert path of inpatient clinical care, where the FDA CDS exemption posture depends on alerts being delivered transparently to clinicians who can review the reasoning, silently-failing scoring is a CDS-posture concern in addition to an operational one.
- **Fix:** Add five SQS DLQs to the Architecture Diagram: `event-normalizer-dlq`, `scoring-orchestrator-dlq`, `alert-router-dlq`, `ack-capture-dlq`, and `outcome-capture-dlq`. Add a Prerequisites note: "Configure each Lambda's `OnFailure` destination to a dedicated SQS DLQ. CloudWatch alarms on DLQ depth alert the on-call clinical-informatics-engineering team; for the event-normalizer-dlq and scoring-orchestrator-dlq specifically, alarm threshold should be 1 because a single dropped event is a patient-state gap and a single unscored patient is a missed-alert-opportunity. Replay events from DLQ after fixing the root cause; for events older than the deterioration prediction window, escalate to clinical-governance-committee review rather than auto-replay because the timing-of-detection is itself part of the system's safety case." Add a "DLQ and replay" bullet to "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" that ties the discipline to the existing disaster-recovery paragraph.

#### Finding A3: Treatment-Leakage and Feature-Cutoff Discipline Identified in Prose but Not Enforced in Pseudocode

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture / Pedagogy
- **Location:** "The Technology" Sampling-Time-Windows-And-Right-Censoring subsection (Treatment leakage paragraph: "A patient who got broad-spectrum antibiotics started at hour 18 of their stay may not deteriorate further because the antibiotics worked. The model that predicts deterioration without knowing antibiotics were started will look like it predicted survival; the model that knows about the antibiotics is at risk of learning 'antibiotics → no deterioration' which isn't quite right. This is the fundamental 'treatment effect on prediction' problem, and the cleanest mitigations are either restricting the model to features available at decision time only, or framing the problem as causal inference (not what most production deterioration models do)"); "The Technology" Event-Leakage paragraph; Step 4 `compute_features` pseudocode.
- **Problem:** The recipe's prose identifies treatment leakage and event leakage as fundamental modeling concerns. The Event-Leakage paragraph correctly names the 30-60 minute lookahead boundary as essential. The Treatment-Leakage paragraph correctly names the "restrict the model to features available at decision time only" mitigation. This is exactly right.

  But Step 4's `compute_features` pseudocode does not enforce the feature-cutoff discipline:
  - It does not specify an `as_of` cutoff that excludes features whose `recorded_at` or `observed_at` is after the prediction time. The function signature `compute_features(patient_id, encounter_id, as_of)` accepts an `as_of` parameter but the `state.current_vitals.get(vital_code)` and `state.recent_labs.get(lab_code)` reads from the current patient state, which is always the latest state at the time of the call. There is no temporal filtering.
  - The `has_active_antibiotic`, `has_active_vasopressor`, `has_active_opioid`, etc. medication-class features include any administration in the recent-medication window, including administrations that may have occurred after a triggering event but before the scoring call ran. For an event-driven score that fires on a new vital observation, the medication state at scoring time may include administrations that were given between the triggering event's `observed_at` and the scoring call's actual execution time. This is a subtle form of data leakage.
  - The `recent_lactate_order`, `recent_blood_culture_order`, `recent_oxygen_titration` order-context features are correctly framed in the prose ("New orders ... often precede formal deterioration recognition by hours, because the bedside team is acting on suspicion before they call rapid response. The 'the team has just ordered a lactate' feature is sometimes one of the highest-importance features in production models"). But this same prose names the concern that order patterns reflect the team's clinical reasoning, which is causally entangled with the outcome the model is trying to predict. A model trained with these features and deployed without proper leakage guards will learn "the team ordered a lactate → high risk" which is the right correlation but a fragile one (a hospital that changes its lactate-ordering policy will produce model drift that's not in the patient population).

  A reader who treats the pseudocode as the implementation specification will produce a system that does not enforce the as-of cutoff that the prose identifies as essential. The recipe's audience includes ML engineers who may construct training datasets based on the recipe's framing; skipping the as-of-cutoff discipline at the feature-engine level produces models with optimistic retrospective AUROC that will not generalize to production.

  Same prose-vs-pseudocode asymmetry pattern as Recipes 3.4 A3, 3.5 A3 and A4, 3.6 A3.
- **Fix:** Two coordinated edits:
  1. Update Step 4 `compute_features` to explicitly enforce the as-of cutoff. The function should fetch from a point-in-time view of the patient state (DynamoDB GetItem with a `consistent_read` for the latest state, then filter every field by `observed_at <= as_of` in-memory; or use the time-travel-capable variant of the state store if available). Every field read should be temporally bounded:
     ```
     // Current vitals: only those observed at or before as_of.
     FOR each vital_code in CORE_VITALS_CODES:
         latest = state.current_vitals.get(vital_code)
         IF latest is not null AND latest.observed_at <= as_of:
             features[f"vital_{vital_code}_current"] = latest.value
             features[f"vital_{vital_code}_age_minutes"] = minutes_between(latest.observed_at, as_of)
         ELSE:
             features[f"vital_{vital_code}_current"] = null
             features[f"vital_{vital_code}_age_minutes"] = null
     // Medications: only administrations strictly before as_of, with optional configurable lookahead-buffer.
     active_meds = [m for m in state.recent_medications IF m.administered_at <= as_of - LEAKAGE_BUFFER_MINUTES]
     ```
     Add a `LEAKAGE_BUFFER_MINUTES` configuration constant (typically 30-60 minutes per the Event-Leakage paragraph) that excludes feature data observed within the buffer window before `as_of`. Add to the Step 4 prose: "The leakage buffer is a clinical-governance-owned configuration: too small a buffer admits features that reflect the team's response to the deterioration the model is trying to predict; too large a buffer admits stale features that don't reflect the patient's current state. 30-60 minutes is the typical operating range; specific value is per local clinical workflow."
  2. Add a paragraph to the General Architecture Pattern's "Feature Engine" subsection naming feature-cutoff and leakage-buffer discipline as a first-class concern: "The feature engine produces a feature vector as of a specified prediction time. Every field is temporally bounded: vitals, labs, medication administrations, order events. Features that reflect post-prediction-time state (a lactate result that came back after the scoring window's prediction time; an antibiotic administered after the triggering event but before the scoring call ran) are excluded by an explicit leakage-buffer filter. The training pipeline uses the same feature engine with the same leakage-buffer setting against historical data, so the offline-online consistency the SageMaker Feature Store enforces also enforces leakage discipline."

#### Finding A4: Cold-Start Handling Identified in "Where It Struggles" but No Architectural Primitive in Pseudocode

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture / Pedagogy
- **Location:** "Where it struggles" subsection ("Cold-start patients. A patient just admitted has no prior vitals, no patient-specific baseline, and no trajectory features.   4. Add a paragraph to the General Architecture Pattern's "Scoring Service" subsection naming cold-start handling as a first-class concern with the population-prior-or-NEWS2 fallback pattern.

#### Finding A5: Suppression-Rule Expiry Mentioned in Pseudocode but Not Enforced

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture / Pedagogy
- **Location:** Step 7 `check_suppression_rules` (`IF state.suppression_until AND NOW() < state.suppression_until: return { suppressed: true, ... }`); the pseudocode shows the suppression-check at flag-emission time but no expiry-enforcement primitive.

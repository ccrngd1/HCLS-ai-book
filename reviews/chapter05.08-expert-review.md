## Overall Assessment

This is the eighth recipe in Chapter 5 and the chapter's second Complex-tier recipe (four-star complexity). It introduces the recipe-specific concepts that distinguish it from the prior entity-resolution recipes (5.1 patient, 5.2 provider, 5.3 address, 5.4 eligibility, 5.5 cross-facility, 5.6 claims-clinical, 5.7 longitudinal-name-change): the matcher operates on cryptographically-transformed proxies of demographic features rather than on the features themselves; the privacy guarantee is a multi-organization contract enforced by cryptography rather than by BAA alone; the trust architecture (who is trusted to hold what) is a first-class architectural primitive that the protocol selection materializes; the salt is the cryptographic root-of-trust whose custody, rotation, and audit posture are load-bearing for the entire privacy claim; the accuracy ceiling is genuinely lower than direct matching and the institution has to choose use cases where the trade-off is acceptable; the operational rhythm includes salt rotation, parameterization upgrade, re-identification-risk review, and consent-withdrawal handling as recurring events rather than one-time concerns; and the regulatory context (post-Dobbs state-law overlays, 42 CFR Part 2, 21st Century Cures Act information-blocking, GDPR / PIPEDA / U.K. DPA cross-border applicability, the CMS Patient Access API ecosystem maturing into patient-mediated linkage) is more directly load-bearing than in any prior recipe in the chapter. The opening research-conference vignette earns its position: two people sitting at the same table for forty-five minutes about a GLP-1-and-MACE outcomes question with the clinical data at the academic medical center and the pharmacy fill data at the regional payer, neither party legally able to hand the other a flat file of demographics, both knowing the answer is in the intersection, sets up the cascade of operational vignettes (multi-site clinical trial pharmacy-fill verification, state cancer registry social-determinant-and-mortality-detail enrichment, ACO out-of-network ED visit attribution, precision-oncology research consortium across twenty academic medical centers and a sequencing vendor and tumor registries, post-state-law within-department immunization-registry-to-hospitalization-registry vaccine-effectiveness analysis, TEFCA QHIN-of-QHINs national-scale Medicare-Medicaid linkage, pharmaceutical-real-world-evidence platform with commercial tokenizer integration) at exactly the right level of "this is what privacy-preserving record linkage actually looks like in production" energy.

The Technology section's "The Conventional Matcher Versus the Privacy-Preserving Matcher" subsection is the chapter's clearest articulation of what changes when the disclosure is constrained by cryptography rather than by a BAA. The "Three Families of PPRL Protocols" subsection (trusted-third-party tokenization model, Bloom-filter-based encoding model with CLK construction, secure-multi-party-computation and private-set-intersection model with garbled circuits, secret-sharing, oblivious transfer, homomorphic encryption, zero-knowledge proofs) is correct and at the right grain, with the operational-economics framing (most production deployments are tokenizer or Bloom-filter; SMPC is for special cases at small volumes) exactly right. The "What the Bloom Filter Encoding Actually Does" walk-through (bigram tokenization, k cryptographic hash functions parameterized by a shared salt, bit positions in a 1024-bit array, Sørensen-Dice coefficient on the encoded filters, the ~0.94 / ~0.85 / ~0.18 calibration examples for "John Smith" vs "Jon Smith" / "Jane Smith" / "Maria Garcia") is the recipe's strongest single passage of cryptographic pedagogy in the chapter. The "What the Matching Layer Has to Capture" eight-dimension enumeration (shared cryptographic salt and parameterization, protocol-specific trust architecture, linkage-result-disclosure policy, re-identification risk model, consent-and-purpose-of-use governance, linkage-performance-monitoring discipline, configuration-versioning and re-encoding lifecycle, cross-protocol bridge for hybrid deployments) is correct and recipe-specific. The "Why It Is Harder Than It Sounds" six-bullet enumeration (accuracy ceiling genuinely lower than direct matching, trust architecture non-trivial to negotiate, cryptographic-key management operationally heavier, protocol-execution coordination has its own failure modes, linkage results are themselves PHI in some scenarios, cohort-stratified accuracy disparities harder to detect and to fix) is correct and recipe-specific. The "Where the Field Has Moved" subsection (open-source toolkits maturing with `anonlink` / `clkhash` / `OpenMined PSI` / `Microsoft SEAL` / `MP-SPDZ`, commercial tokenization consolidating with Datavant / HealthVerity / IQVIA, differential privacy as complementary primitive, TEE hardware enabling new patterns with Nitro Enclaves and SGX and SEV-SNP and Azure Confidential Computing, regulatory expectations tightening with Safe Harbor and Expert Determination and post-Dobbs and information-blocking, patient-mediated PPRL emerging through CMS Patient Access API) is correctly granular and forward-looking.

The six-stage architecture (prepare the demographic features for encoding, execute the agreed cryptographic encoding under the shared protocol parameterization, exchange the encoded data among participants under the trust architecture, run the matcher against the encoded data, persist the linkage results with disclosure-policy and audit metadata, react to events that supersede the linkage) is the right shape for the problem. The encoding-step elevation as the load-bearing addition versus recipe 5.5 ("a conventional cross-facility matcher operates on plaintext demographic features that flow through the HIE's matching layer. The PPRL matcher operates on cryptographically-encoded representations that are produced before any cross-organizational exchange") is the recipe's strongest single architectural primitive. Threshold calibration as protocol-specific (separate from recipe 5.5's plaintext thresholds; PPRL similarity scores are systematically lower for the same underlying record pair because of encoding noise) is correctly framed as discipline rather than convenience. Trust architecture as a first-class architectural concern ("the matcher's output is only as private as the protocol's trust assumption holds") is the right elevation. Disclosure-policy granularity (per-record-match-flags, intersection-count, k-anonymous-aggregate, differentially-private-aggregate, encrypted-match-indicator) as a multi-form mechanism the architecture supports simultaneously is recipe-specific and correctly architected. Cohort-stratified accuracy monitoring with privacy-preserving cohort-axis derivation (per-participant locally-computed cohort-axis hashes that the participants contribute in the encoding step; the matcher stratifies accuracy without learning the underlying axis values) is the recipe's strongest single operational primitive on the equity dimension. ### Finding S1: Per-Participant Encoding Path, Cross-Account Exchange Path, Matcher Endpoint Attestation Path, Salt-Rotation Ceremony, Consent-Withdrawal Path, Patient-Mediated PPRL Trigger, Cross-Recipe Invalidation Lambda, and EventBridge Fan-Out Lack Identity-Boundary Specification With Recipe-Specific Cryptographic-Root-of-Trust Stakes

- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Expert:** Security (PHI integrity boundary, cryptographic-root-of-trust integrity, authorization, regulatory)
- **Location:** AWS Implementation Lambda paragraph: "Each participating organization's encoding Lambda is invoked per-record (or per-batch) with the standardized record payload, the protocol parameterization version, the salt reference, and the consent-posture metadata." Architecture diagram shows `EB1 -->|FanOut| C5[Recipe 5.5 cross-facility matcher]` through `C9[Per-participant operational systems]` plus the salt-custody flow `SC1[(AWS CloudHSM salt custody)] --> SC2[(AWS KMS per-cycle key derivation)] -->|salt-key-version| PA3 / PB3 / LE1`. Step 6 `invalidate_on_event(invalidation_event)` reads `invalidation_event.source` and branches on `salt_rotation` / `parameterization_upgrade` / `consent_withdrawal` / `identity_merge_recipe_5_1` / `name_change_recipe_5_7` / `re_identification_risk_model_update` without specified producer-signed-envelope verification. Variations / Patient-mediated PPRL paragraph: "the app, acting as the patient's agent, retrieves encoded records from each participating organization's API endpoint and orchestrates the matching at the patient's chosen linkage-execution endpoint." The recipe's own TODO at the IAM Permissions row (referenced as "Same chapter pattern as 5.1, 5.4, 5.5, 5.6, 5.7") explicitly enumerates the recipe-specific extensions with line-by-line specifications but the architecture text does not actually specify the boundary.

- **Problem:** The recipe specifies the PPRL pipeline at flow-and-service granularity but is silent on the identity-boundary policy that controls who can invoke each path and what proves the caller is authorized to act on a particular linkage cycle, encoded payload, or salt-related operation. The chapter-wide pattern from 5.1 through 5.7 has converged on a structured identity-boundary specification; Recipe 5.8 inherits the concern with eight concrete attack surfaces, several of which are uniquely sharp here:

  1. **The per-participant encoding Lambda accepts the protocol parameterization version, the salt reference, and the consent-posture metadata.** A forged event (an attacker-controlled `parameterization_version` claim, a compromised salt-reference that points to a salt-key version the attacker controls, a consent-posture claim that bypasses the consent filter) silently produces encoded payloads under the wrong parameterization or salt or with records the patient did not consent to include. The producer-signed envelope pattern (the chapter pattern: `source_system`, `source_record_id`, `event_id`, `signed_payload`, `signature` with consumer-side signature validation) is not specified. The recipe's TODO names this as "the per-participant encoding Lambda receives a producer-signed envelope" but the architecture text does not actually specify it.

  2. **The cross-account encoded-records exchange path varies by trust architecture.** The tokenizer model uploads to a third-party tokenizer's S3 bucket with cross-account access policies; the linkage-broker model uploads to a designated linkage-broker; the TEE-attested-endpoint model requires the participants to verify the enclave's attestation before uploading; the SMPC protocol runner engages in a multi-round protocol exchange under mTLS-and-attested-execution verification at each round. Each transport type has its own boundary properties. A misconfigured exchange (the participant uploads to a counterparty's bucket without verifying the cross-account policy enumerates the participant's role; the participant accepts a TEE attestation document without verifying the expected measurement; the participant participates in an SMPC round without verifying the counterparty's attested-execution document) silently routes encoded data to a destination the protocol's privacy claim does not cover. The recipe's TODO names "the cross-account encoded-records exchange is authenticated through cross-account bucket policies that enumerate the specific roles authorized to read or write, plus mTLS-and-PrivateLink for the network transport" but the architecture text does not specify the enumeration, the verification flow, or the rejection semantics.

  3. **The matcher Lambda or Nitro Enclave role is the most consequential per its name.** The matcher consumes encoded data from every participant's exchange bucket and produces the linkage decisions that flow to the disclosure step. A misconfigured matcher role (it has access to a participant's exchange bucket outside the cycle's active execution window; it has access to a non-attested compute environment; it has time-unbound access to the encoded data) silently exfiltrates encoded payloads or runs the matcher under conditions the protocol's privacy claim does not cover. The recipe's TODO names "the matcher Lambda or Nitro Enclave role is per-cycle bound through Step Functions and has time-bound access to the encoded data only during the cycle's active execution window" but the architecture text does not specify the time-bound access, the per-cycle binding, or the cycle-completion-cleanup.

  4. **The salt-rotation ceremony is the most consequential single event in the entire pipeline.** The salt is the cryptographic root-of-trust; if the salt is compromised, the privacy guarantee of every encoded record under that salt is retroactively broken. The recipe correctly elevates the salt-custody pattern (HSM, no-single-participant-unilateral-access, key policies, audit logging) but does not specify the rotation ceremony's identity-boundary policy. A forged rotation event (an attacker-controlled `new_salt_key_version` claim, a single-actor approval that bypasses dual-control, a timing attack that completes the rotation before the audit-log replication catches up) silently substitutes a salt the attacker knows or invalidates the entire encoded-data corpus. The recipe's TODO names "the salt-rotation ceremony is dual-controlled at the architectural level (two HSM operators from non-overlapping organizations must approve a rotation operation; the rotation is audit-logged with both operator identities and the new salt-key-version reference)" but the architecture text does not specify the dual-control approval mechanism, the HSM-operator identity binding, or the audit-log replication SLA.

  5. **The consent-withdrawal path is the dignity-and-trust read path.** A patient who withdraws consent for inclusion in the linkage triggers an update to the participant's local consent store and a notification to downstream consumers per the protocol's policy on retroactive handling. A forged consent-withdrawal event (an attacker-controlled `source_record_id` claim that withdraws the wrong patient's consent; a forged restoration that re-includes a patient who has actually withdrawn) silently misrepresents the patient's preference. The recipe's TODO names "the consent-withdrawal path requires patient re-authentication and is audit-logged with explicit 'patient-mediated' attribution" but the architecture text does not specify the patient-authentication path, the per-patient binding, or the audit-attribution discipline.

  6. **The patient-mediated PPRL trigger source is patient-authenticated rather than system-authenticated.** A patient who connects her records to a personal-health-record app authorizes the app to retrieve encoded records from each participating organization's API endpoint and orchestrate the matching on her behalf. This is a different authentication model than any prior recipe in the chapter (5.1 through 5.6 are system-to-system or system-to-payer; 5.7 introduced patient-portal-app authentication for name-change updates; 5.8 extends it to the linkage-execution authority). The Variations section names the pattern but does not architect the authentication flow, the per-patient authorization-to-source-record-id binding at each participant, the abuse-prevention rate-limiting (one patient cannot drive arbitrary linkage cycles for unrelated patients), the audit posture for patient-mediated linkages, or the rejection semantics when the patient's authentication does not bind to a known identity at one of the participants.

  7. **The cross-recipe invalidation Lambda accepts events from multiple recipe-external producers.** Salt-rotation events come from the salt-custody system; parameterization-upgrade events come from the protocol-parameterization governance flow; consent-withdrawal events come from the patient-portal flow; identity-merge events come from recipe 5.1; name-change events come from recipe 5.7; re-identification-risk-model-update events come from the institutional privacy team. Each is a recipe-external producer; the invalidation Lambda has no specified mechanism to validate that the asserted `event.source` matches the actual originating principal. A forged `salt_rotation` event with attacker-controlled `prior_salt_key_version` and `new_salt_key_version` could mass-invalidate prior linkages and substitute a salt the attacker knows; a forged `parameterization_upgrade` event with attacker-controlled `new_parameterization_version` could downgrade the defensive measures retroactively widening re-identification risk; a forged `re_identification_risk_model_update` event could trigger a parameterization rollback that reduces the privacy guarantee.

  8. **The cross-recipe EventBridge fan-out reaches recipe 5.5, recipe 5.6, recipe 5.7, the analytic consumer, and the per-participant operational systems.** The architecture specifies the fan-out but not the producer-signed envelope on the event payload, the consumer-side validation of the asserted source, the schema-version-pinning policy, or the access-control-envelope-aware routing. A forged `pprl_linkage_cycle_completed` event could feed false data into recipe 5.5's cross-facility matcher (producing wrong-cross-organizational identity assertions), into recipe 5.6's claims-clinical linkage (producing wrong-claims-to-clinical attribution), into recipe 5.7's longitudinal-name-change pipeline (producing wrong-historical-name reasoning), or into the analytic consumer (producing wrong-research-output). The recipe's TODO names "the cross-recipe EventBridge fan-out validates producer-signed envelopes at consumers and applies access-control-envelope-aware routing so that consumers in different trust tiers receive different event detail levels" but the architecture text does not specify the routing, the validation, or the trust-tier definition.

  The HIPAA Privacy Rule's minimum-necessary requirement, the multi-party trust framework's privacy-claim integrity, and the cryptographic root-of-trust integrity all depend on the identity boundary. A read of the encoded data that bypasses the cycle-boundary access control is a minimum-necessary violation and a trust-framework breach. ### What's Done Well

- **Six-stage pipeline shape is correct.** Prepare the demographic features for encoding (standardization and normalization), execute the agreed cryptographic encoding under the shared protocol parameterization, exchange the encoded data among participants under the trust architecture, run the matcher against the encoded data, persist the linkage results with the disclosure-policy and audit metadata, react to events that supersede the linkage maps cleanly to the operational reality. The transport-type heterogeneity (tokenizer model, linkage-broker model, TEE-attested-endpoint model with Nitro Enclave attestation, SMPC protocol runner, patient-mediated model) is correctly handled by a single downstream pipeline with transport-aware routing.
- **Encoding-as-load-bearing-addition is correctly elevated.** The recipe's strongest single architectural primitive: "the encoding step is the load-bearing addition versus recipe 5.5. A conventional cross-facility matcher operates on plaintext demographic features that flow through the HIE's matching layer. The PPRL matcher operates on cryptographically-encoded representations that are produced before any cross-organizational exchange. The encoding step is per-participant and parameterization-pinned: every participating organization runs the same encoding under the same parameterization to produce comparable encoded records. Mis-coordinated encoding produces silent linkage failures."
- **Threshold calibration is protocol-specific.** The recipe correctly elevates the separate-from-5.5 calibration ("Re-using the recipe 5.5 thresholds for PPRL produces either too many missed matches ... or too many false acceptances ... The right thresholds are derived from a separate calibration set that includes confirmed cross-organizational matches, near-miss family-member cases, and known-incorrect linkages, all encoded under the same parameterization the production matcher will use"). - **Trust architecture as first-class architectural concern.** "Treating the trust architecture as an architectural artifact (a documented, versioned, audit-capable contract) rather than an informal norm is the discipline that makes PPRL operationally trustworthy." This is the recipe's strongest single observation about why PPRL deployments succeed or fail and earns its position in the General Architecture Pattern.
- **Disclosure-policy granularity matters more than for conventional matching.** "Conventional matching's output is a yes/no match decision. PPRL's output can be a yes/no match decision, an intersection count, a per-cohort aggregate, an encrypted match indicator that only one designated party can decrypt, or a differentially-private summary of the linkage. Each disclosure form has its own privacy properties and its own analytic utility." The architecture supports multiple disclosure forms simultaneously for different use cases on the same encoded data; this is correctly architected.
- **Cohort-stratified accuracy monitoring with privacy-preserving cohort-axis derivation.** The recipe's strongest single operational primitive on the equity dimension: per-participant locally-computed cohort-axis hashes that the participants contribute in the encoding step; the linkage-execution endpoint stratifies the accuracy metrics by the cohort-axis hashes without learning the underlying axis values.

Salt rotation is a coordinated multi-organization event that invalidates all previously-encoded data and requires the participating organizations to re-encode their populations under the new salt." The catch-up-window is correctly identified as both an operational discipline and a privacy concern; the architecture acknowledges the trade-off.
- **Reversibility through re-encoding rather than retraction.** "A linkage that was wrong cannot be retracted from a counterparty that has already consumed it; the only mitigation is to re-encode under a new parameterization, re-run the linkage, and disclose the corrected result, with explicit communication that the prior result is superseded." The architecture treats re-encoding as the primary mechanism for handling linkage errors, with the prior result preserved in the audit trail but explicitly marked as superseded.
- **Consent withdrawal as forward-only.** "A patient who withdraws consent for inclusion in the linkage cannot retroactively un-link records that have already been disclosed; the participating organization removes the patient from future encoding and re-running, but the prior linkage results remain in the consumer's possession. The architecture supports consent withdrawal as a forward-looking event with explicit patient-facing communication about what can and cannot be retracted."

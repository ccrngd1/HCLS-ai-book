## Overall Assessment

This is the seventh recipe in Chapter 5 and the chapter's first Complex-tier recipe (four-star complexity). It introduces the recipe-specific concepts that distinguish it from the prior entity-resolution recipes (5.1 patient, 5.2 provider, 5.3 address, 5.4 eligibility, 5.5 cross-facility, 5.6 claims-clinical): name is no longer a string but a time-varying attribute with effective spans, sources, supporting documents, sensitivity classifications, and access-control envelopes; the matcher operates against a temporal-identity model rather than a flat name field; the load-bearing concern is dignity rather than data quality, with gender-affirming care, intimate-partner-violence relocation, witness-protection, and protective-custody all exercising the sensitivity-class machinery; the access-control envelope (not the matcher) is the system's primary enforcement primitive for who sees what; the historical retrofit problem (decades of records under prior names) is a one-time backfill that is a project rather than a feature; the cross-organizational propagation problem is uniquely unsolvable by the institution alone (the patient-mediated path is becoming the load-bearing pattern); and the regulatory context (21st Century Cures Act information-blocking, HIPAA, post-Dobbs and post-Bostock state-law overlays) is more directly load-bearing for compliance than in any prior recipe in the chapter.

The opening cancer-infusion-center vignette earns its position: the nurse preparing chemotherapy in chair 4 with a patient whose chart has been silently fragmented by a name change ("the chart-rendering layer in the EHR did not consult the cross-reference, or consulted it and applied it incorrectly, or consulted it and rendered the historical chart under the prior name without surfacing the linkage to the nurse, or consulted it and silently dropped the historical chart entirely") sets up the cascade of operational vignettes (women's health clinic with forty-year horizon and over-forty-percent lifetime name-change rate, gender-affirming care service line, state immunization registry, oncology service line with cumulative-anthracycline-dose continuity, Medicare-Advantage-insurance-verification with three-name-variant Medicare card, ACO HEDIS quality measurement, TEFCA QHIN identity exchange, multi-decade outcomes-research cohort refresh) at exactly the right level of "this is what longitudinal name-change matching actually looks like in production" energy. The clinical-safety stakes are correctly elevated without melodrama; the dignity-of-the-patient framing is present without being preachy; the recipe earns the four-star complexity rating it claims.

The Technology section's "Why Names Are Not What You Think They Are" subsection is the chapter's clearest articulation of the temporal-identity-model versus flat-name-field mismatch. The seven-dimension enumeration of what the time-varying-name model has to capture (effective span, source, authority and document references, sensitivity classification, cross-reference linkages, sex and gender as separate time-varying fields, reversibility and superseding events) is correct and at the right grain. The six-bullet "Why It Is Harder Than It Sounds" enumeration (historical records carry only the name current at the time, supporting documents are unavailable for many name changes, family members are a strong confounder more so than in non-temporal matching, sensitivity and patient-autonomy considerations are not negotiable, cross-organizational reach magnifies every failure mode, the retrofit problem is enormous) is correct and recipe-specific. The "Where the Field Has Moved" subsection (gender-affirming care as first-class data-model concern, FHIR's `Patient.name` as a list with `use` and `period` fields, state-level vital-records integration variability, information-blocking obligations applying to records under prior names, equity and disparity monitoring with name-tradition cohort axes, patient-mediated identity through the CMS Patient Access API ecosystem) is correctly granular and forward-looking.

The six-stage architecture (ingest the demographic update or new record, detect a candidate name-change event, evaluate the candidate against the patient's existing identity record, persist the temporal-name update with audit and sensitivity metadata, propagate the linkage to dependent stores, react to events that supersede prior linkages) is the right shape for the problem. The trigger-source heterogeneity (registration update, payer eligibility refresh, vital-records feed, cross-organizational match refresh, document upload, patient-facing app connection, bulk historical-records reconciliation) is correctly handled by a single downstream pipeline with event-aware re-evaluation. The detection step's elevation as the load-bearing addition versus recipe 5.1 ("the temporal model needs an explicit detector because the addition of a new name to an existing identity is structurally different from a new record matching an existing identity; the indirect case is the dominant operational path and is the one that institutions tend to under-invest in") is the recipe's strongest single architectural primitive. Threshold calibration as name-change-specific (not re-using the recipe 5.1 thresholds) is correctly framed as discipline rather than convenience. Sensitivity classification at the prior-name level rather than the identity level is the right model. The split-of-concerns between the matcher (knows the linkage) and the access-control envelope (controls who sees what) is correctly elevated to architectural primitive ("the matcher is not deciding what to display; it is deciding what is true. The display decision lives in the access-control envelope, which is consulted by every consumer that reads the identity record"). Reversibility as the architecture rather than as a feature is the right framing. Document-source strength as a confidence input is correctly recipe-specific. The TransactWriteItems plus outbox pattern in Step 4 pseudocode is a positive structural improvement on the chapter pattern (5.1, 5.2, 5.3, 5.4, 5.5 all carried Finding A1 for the absence of this pattern; recipes 5.6 and 5.7 specify it).

### Finding S1: Per-Event Detection / Resolution Lambda, Invalidation Lambda, Read API, Cross-Recipe EventBridge Fan-Out, Patient-Portal-App Trigger, Vital-Records Feed Trigger, Document-Upload Trigger, and Sensitivity-Class-Update Path Lack Identity-Boundary Specification

- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Expert:** Security (PHI integrity boundary, patient-dignity boundary, authorization, regulatory)
- **Location:** AWS Implementation Lambda paragraph: "When a registration update arrives with a different name from the existing identity, when a payer-eligibility refresh detects a name discrepancy, when a vital-records feed delivers a name-change event, when a document upload provides supporting evidence, the per-event Lambda invokes the detector, the resolver, and the persistence step on a single identity. Each invocation is in VPC with VPC endpoints for downstream services." Architecture diagram shows `EB1 -->|FanOut| C5[Local MPI recipe 5.1]` through `C12[Quality and Risk-Adjustment Pipelines]` plus `H1[Amazon HealthLake]`. Step 6 `invalidate_on_event(invalidation_event)` reads `invalidation_event.source` and branches on it without specified producer-signed-envelope verification. The recipe's own TODO at the IAM Permissions row (referenced as "Same chapter pattern as 5.1, 5.4, 5.5, 5.6") names the chapter pattern but the architecture text does not actually specify the boundary.

- **Problem:** The recipe specifies the longitudinal-name-change pipeline at flow-and-service granularity but is silent on the identity-boundary policy that controls who can invoke each path and what proves the caller is authorized to act on a particular identity record. The chapter-wide pattern from 5.1 through 5.6 has converged on a structured identity-boundary specification; Recipe 5.7 inherits the concern with eight concrete attack surfaces, several of which are uniquely sharp here:

  1. **The patient-portal-app trigger source is patient-authenticated rather than system-authenticated.** A patient who connects her records to a personal-health-record app authorizes the app to retrieve records from organizations that knew her under her prior name; the app does the linkage on her behalf with her authentication serving as the strong identifier. This is a different authentication model than any prior recipe in the chapter (5.1 through 5.6 are all system-to-system or system-to-payer). The recipe's TODO at the IAM Permissions row names "separate Cognito-federated authentication for the patient-portal-app trigger source" but the architecture text does not specify the authentication path, the per-patient authorization-to-identity-id binding, the abuse-prevention rate-limiting (one patient cannot drive arbitrary identity-update events for unrelated patients), the audit posture for patient-mediated changes, or the rejection semantics when the patient's authentication does not bind to a known identity. A misconfigured patient-portal-app trigger source produces patient-impersonation attacks that the institution cannot detect.

  2. **The vital-records feed trigger source is cross-organizational.** The state vital-records agency is an external organization with its own data-use agreement, its own authentication infrastructure, and its own audit posture. The recipe correctly elevates the integration to a sub-project ("the partner-agreement, the network connectivity, the data-format normalization, the privacy-and-purpose-of-use constraints, the audit requirements") but does not specify the per-feed authentication, the producer-signed envelope, or the consumer-side validation that the asserted source matches the contracted agency. A forged vital-records-feed event with an attacker-controlled `event.identity_id` and `event.new_name` would produce a STRONG-source-classified resolution that auto-resolves at HIGH confidence, with audit attribution to the state agency rather than to the attacker.

  3. **The document-upload trigger source carries the legal-change supporting evidence.** A court order PDF, a marriage certificate scan, a divorce decree, or a driver's-license verification, once uploaded, promotes a name-change event from REVIEW_PENDING to AUTO_RESOLVE_HIGH and adjusts the resolution's effective date to the document-extracted legal-change date. A forged document upload (an attacker who has compromised a registration-staff workstation, or a patient who uploads a fraudulent document, or a document-upload API endpoint without producer-signed-envelope verification) silently produces a STRONG-source resolution with the wrong patient binding or the wrong effective date. The audit trail attributes the resolution to "automated" with the document reference; tracing the cause back to the forged upload requires the document's own provenance, which the architecture does not specify.

  4. **The sensitivity-class-update path is the most consequential per its name.** A patient who initially classified a name change as GENERAL and later upgrades the classification to GENDER_AFFIRMING (with masked-display preference) is the architecturally happy case; a forged update that downgrades a GENDER_AFFIRMING / WITNESS_PROTECTION / IPV_RELOCATION classification to GENERAL retroactively widens the access-control envelope and produces dignity-and-safety failures the patient cannot recover from. The recipe correctly elevates the asymmetry ("The architecture cannot retract disclosures that have already left the institution, but it can update the access-control envelope for future disclosures") but does not specify the producer-signed-envelope verification on sensitivity-class-update events, the patient-authentication path for patient-initiated updates, or the dual-control requirement for staff-initiated downgrades.

  5. **The invalidation Lambda accepts events from 5.1 (identity-merge), 5.4 (eligibility-change-affecting-cross-reference), 5.5 (cross-organizational-identity-change), 5.6 (claims-clinical-link-invalidation), the vocabulary-store (annual coding-update for sex / gender or naming-tradition data), the document-store (document-strength-upgrade), and the patient-preference store (sensitivity-class-update).** Each is a recipe-external producer; the invalidation Lambda has no specified mechanism to validate that the asserted `event.source` matches the actual originating Lambda role. A forged `identity_merge` event with attacker-controlled `merged_into_identity_id` could fold one patient's name history into another's, producing wrong-patient clinical-record visibility at scale. A forged `cross_facility_match_invalidated` event could selectively retract prior-name handling for query responses.

  6. **The per-event detection / resolution Lambda accepts events from upstream sources.** A forged event (an attacker-controlled `trigger_event.local_patient_id`, a compromised feed-ingestion source replaying old events, an event with a `source_type` claim swapped to "court_order" to upgrade source-strength to STRONG) silently produces a name-change resolution with the wrong patient binding or the wrong source-strength weighting. The producer-signed envelope pattern (the chapter pattern: `source_system`, `source_record_id`, `event_id`, `signed_payload`, `signature` with consumer-side signature validation) is not specified.

  7. **The read API for downstream consumers exposes parsed identity records and (potentially) the underlying audit payloads, including the prior-name history.** The architecture says "the read API for downstream consumers" but does not specify the API Gateway resource policy, the WAF rules, the per-caller authorization-to-identity-id binding, or the sensitivity-aware response shaping (different audiences see different views; chart-rendering consumers may see the prior names, release-of-information consumers may see only the current name unless the access-control envelope authorizes prior-name disclosure, research consumers may see the linked identity with no name detail at all). Without architectural specification, a single endpoint serves all views and the sensitivity-classification machinery becomes presentation-time hope rather than architectural guarantee.

  8. **The cross-recipe EventBridge fan-out reaches nine consumers.** The architecture specifies the fan-out but not the producer-signed envelope on the event payload, the consumer-side validation of the asserted source, the schema-version-pinning policy, or the access-control-envelope-aware routing (the recipe's pseudocode mentions `derive_event_type_for_emission` returning `identity_name_change_resolved_restricted` for high-sensitivity cases, but the consumer-side handling of the restricted variant is not specified; some consumers may need to receive the restricted event but not the prior-name detail; some consumers may need to be excluded from the fan-out entirely for restricted classes). A forged `identity_name_change_resolved` event could propagate a wrong name change to all nine consumers; a forged `identity_sensitivity_updated` event with a downgrade payload could retroactively widen disclosures.

  The HIPAA Privacy Rule's minimum-necessary requirement and the 21st Century Cures Act information-blocking rules both depend on the identity boundary. A read of the identity record that exposes a patient's prior name to a caller without a need-to-know is a minimum-necessary violation and (for sensitivity-classified prior names) a dignity failure that the patient experiences directly. A write or invalidation event that succeeds without proving the originating principal does not have the audit-trail attribution that compliance and patient-trust both require. A sensitivity-class-downgrade event that succeeds without dual control is a uniquely consequential failure mode that no prior recipe in the chapter has at this severity.

  Same regulatory ground as Recipe 5.1 / 5.2 / 5.3 / 5.4 / 5.5 / 5.6 Finding S1; the chapter editor should consolidate identity-check guidance into a chapter preface in the next pass since the same finding now applies across 4.4-5.7. For 5.7 specifically, the patient-portal-app trigger source's patient-authentication model and the sensitivity-class-update path's dignity-and-safety stakes earn the HIGH severity.

- **Fix:** Promote the recipe's own TODO content into the architecture text. Specify the identity-boundary policy and the rejection semantics at the architectural level the chapter has converged on. For the per-event detection / resolution Lambda, specify that the inbound event carries a producer-signed envelope (`source_system`, `source_record_id`, `event_id`, `signed_payload`, `signature`); the Lambda validates the signature against the producer's known signing key (rotated per the institutional secret-rotation policy), validates the source_system is in the allow-list, validates the event_id is unique within a sliding window (idempotency), and rejects events that fail any of these checks with a logged metric and routing to the rejected-events DLQ. Per-source rate limits are specified so a runaway batch-reconciliation job cannot consume the per-event capacity.

  For the patient-portal-app trigger source, specify a separate Cognito-federated authentication path with the patient's own credentials, a per-patient authorization-to-identity-id binding enforced at the Lambda authorizer (one patient cannot drive identity-update events for unrelated patients), per-patient rate limits below the operational-session capacity, and audit logging on every patient-mediated change with explicit "patient-mediated" attribution distinct from "automated" and "reviewer-confirmed."

  For the vital-records feed trigger source, specify the per-agency mTLS authentication, the producer-signed envelope on each event, the consumer-side validation that the asserted source matches the contracted agency, and the per-agency-and-per-event audit logging with the state-agency reference identifier.

  For the document-upload trigger source, specify the per-document provenance chain (uploader identity, upload timestamp, document-hash, document-type-extraction-engine version, legal-change-date-extraction confidence), the dual-control requirement when the document-extraction confidence falls below the auto-resolve threshold, and the audit logging of every document-strength-promotion event with full provenance.

  For the sensitivity-class-update path, specify the dual-control requirement for downgrades (a patient-initiated downgrade requires re-authentication of the patient through the patient-portal flow with explicit confirmation; a staff-initiated downgrade requires two reviewer signatures from the patient-experience-and-dignity committee; the architecture rejects single-actor downgrades), the producer-signed envelope on the update event, the audit logging of every classification change with full evidence, and the explicit forward-only-disclosure-update framing (the architecture cannot retract disclosures that have already left the institution; the patient-facing communication makes this explicit).

  For the invalidation Lambda, specify the producer-signed envelope and the per-event-source allow-list (the `identity_merge` events come only from the 5.1 Lambda's execution role; the `cross_facility_match_invalidated` events come only from the 5.5 Lambda's execution role; etc.), with the consumer-side signature validation and the rejection-on-mismatch metric.

  For the read API, specify an API Gateway with WAF, mTLS for system-to-system clients, the per-caller authorization-to-identity-id binding enforced at the Lambda authorizer or the Lambda layer, the sensitivity-aware response shaping (the response varies by access-control-envelope evaluation: chart-rendering consumers see authorized prior names, release-of-information consumers see the access-control-envelope-permitted release scopes, research consumers see linked identity with no name detail), and the explicit audit logging on every prior-name disclosure with the access-control-envelope state at the time of disclosure.

  For the cross-recipe EventBridge fan-out, specify the producer-signed envelope on the event payload, the consumer-side validation that the asserted `source` matches the expected signing principal, and the access-control-envelope-aware routing (consumers receiving only events for non-restricted sensitivity classes versus consumers receiving all events but honoring the envelope when consuming).

  Reference Recipe 5.1 / 5.2 / 5.3 / 5.4 / 5.5 / 5.6 Finding S1 as the chapter pattern.

### Finding S2: Audit-Log Retention Specified With Generic "Regulatory Retention Floor" Without Explicit Floor (Chapter-Wide Pattern, Already TODO'd, Recipe-Specific Audit Volume Implications)

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Security (regulatory, audit, forensic)
- **Location:** Prerequisites CloudTrail row: "CloudTrail logs encrypted with KMS and retained per the regulatory floor." Why-These-Services / S3 paragraph: "The retention floor is typically the longer of the institution's general medical-records retention, the document-specific retention floor where applicable, and the regulatory retention floor for identity-related records." Honest Take's audit-volume paragraph: "the audit-retention costs are non-trivial at population scale. Plan the audit-storage tier explicitly (S3 Glacier Deep Archive after 90 days is typical), the per-class retention floor explicitly, and the audit-query path explicitly so it does not collide with the operational read path." The recipe's own inline TODO at the CloudTrail row (referenced as "Same chapter pattern as 5.1, 5.4, 5.5, 5.6") explicitly names the chapter-pattern fix.
- **Problem:** Same chapter pattern as 5.1 / 5.2 / 5.3 / 5.4 / 5.5 / 5.6 Finding S2. The TODO correctly identifies that the floor should be the longest of (HIPAA 7-year minimum, identity-document retention floor where applicable, state medical-records-retention, research IRB retention where applicable). For 5.7 specifically, four recipe-specific retention concerns, two of which are unique to this recipe:

  1. **Sensitivity-classified events generate substantially more audit volume than general ones.** The Honest Take correctly elevates this: "Every disclosure of the prior name is logged. Every query that touches the prior name is logged." ## Architecture Expert Review

### What's Done Well

- **Six-stage pipeline shape is correct.** Ingest the demographic update or new record, detect a candidate name-change event, evaluate the candidate against the existing identity record, persist the temporal-name update with audit and sensitivity metadata, propagate the linkage to dependent stores, react to events that supersede prior linkages maps cleanly to the operational reality. The trigger-source heterogeneity (registration update, payer eligibility refresh, vital-records feed, cross-organizational match refresh, document upload, patient-facing app connection, bulk historical-records reconciliation) is correctly handled by a single downstream pipeline with event-aware re-evaluation.
- **Detection-as-load-bearing-addition is correctly elevated.** The recipe's strongest single architectural primitive: "the temporal model needs an explicit detector because the addition of a new name to an existing identity is structurally different from a new record matching an existing identity. The detector handles both the direct case (an explicit assertion of a name change) and the indirect case (a high-demographic-match record arriving with a different name from the matched identity). The indirect case is the dominant operational path and is the one that institutions tend to under-invest in."
- **Threshold calibration is name-change-specific.** The recipe correctly elevates the separate-from-5.1 calibration ("Re-using the recipe 5.1 thresholds for name-change decisions produces either too many false-merges ... or too many missed name changes ... The right thresholds for name-change events are derived from a separate calibration set"). DIRECT and INDIRECT thresholds are separately specified; the calibration discipline is institutional rather than magic-number.
- **Sensitivity classification at the prior-name level rather than the identity level.** "A patient may have multiple name changes over a lifetime, with different sensitivity classifications for each. A divorce that reverts to a maiden name may have no special sensitivity, while a subsequent legal name change associated with gender transition has explicit privacy preferences. The model carries the classification at the prior-name level, not at the identity level, because a single identity can have prior names with different sensitivity treatments." This is the right granularity.
- **Matcher-versus-access-control-envelope split-of-concerns.** The recipe correctly elevates this to architectural primitive: "the matcher always knows the linkage between current and prior names; specific users may or may not see the prior name based on their role, the sensitivity classification, the access purpose, and the patient's preferences. Hiding the linkage from the system itself defeats the matcher's job; hiding the prior name from the user surface is a presentation-and-access-control concern. The two are distinct."
- **Patient-self-assertion as a valid but weaker source.** The matcher accepts patient-self-assertion as a name-change source but treats it differently from document-backed assertions, with explicit pending-state and document-eventually-arrives pathway support.
- **Reversibility as the architecture rather than as a feature.** "A name-change event can be wrong, and the architecture has to support cleanly retracting it. The temporal-name record carries an event history, not just a current state; superseding events update the computed view without losing the underlying history; downstream consumers receive invalidation events that allow them to refresh derived state."
- **Multi-source invalidation pipeline.** Step 6's enumeration of invalidation sources (correction, reversal, identity merge, identity unmerge, sensitivity-class update, document-strength upgrade, cross-organizational match invalidation) is comprehensive and operationally complete. The multi-source design accommodates the recipe's recognition that name changes have a multi-decade lifecycle with multiple opportunities for the prior decision to require update.
- **TransactWriteItems plus outbox pattern is specified in Step 4 pseudocode.** This is a positive structural improvement on the chapter pattern: 5.1, 5.2, 5.3, 5.4, 5.5 all had a HIGH finding (Finding A1 in those reviews) for sequential write-without-transactional-wrapping; recipes 5.6 and 5.7 specify the pattern in the pseudocode. The outbox-drainer flow at Step 4D is correctly referenced.
- **Append-only event log with computed current state.** "The record is an append-only event in the identity's history; the current state is computed from the event log. This pattern lets the resolver re-derive the current state under any historical configuration (which is what the audit layer needs for forensic-reconstruction queries)." This is the right pattern for the multi-decade lifecycle the recipe targets.
- **Reference-data-version tracking on every name-change event.** The recipe's repeated framing that every name-change resolution references the matcher_config_version, the nickname-dictionary version, the surname-change-pattern-models version, and the transliteration-maps version active at decision time is the right discipline for forensic reconstruction across reference-data-update cycles.
- **Cross-organizational propagation as a non-fully-solvable problem.** The Honest Take correctly elevates this: "most institutions cannot fully solve this on their own. A name change recorded at organization A propagates to organization B only through one of three pathways: the patient updates her record at organization B in person, the cross-facility refresh path detects the discrepancy at query time, or the patient-mediated flow propagates the change through a personal-health-record app." Each pathway has its own latency and coverage gaps. The patient-mediated path as strategic investment is the right framing.
- **Historical retrofit as project rather than feature.** "Standing up the longitudinal-matching pipeline involves a one-time backfill that re-evaluates years of historical records against whatever name-change evidence is available." The Honest Take's elevation of patient-portal-driven confirmation pathway and conservative thresholds during backfill is the right operational discipline.
- **Configuration-as-data discipline.** The recipe consistently treats matcher_config_version, reference-data versions, threshold values, and sensitivity-classification rules as versioned configuration rather than hardcoded constants. Name-change events reference the versions active at decision time.
- **Why-This-Isn't-Production-Ready section names ten gaps.** Patient-preference UI and consent capture, reference-data sourcing and maintenance, threshold calibration and approval governance, three review queues with sensitivity-aware tooling, information-blocking compliance posture, cross-organizational propagation policy, historical backfill plan and execution, vital-records integration where available, idempotency and retry semantics, cohort-stratified accuracy monitoring discipline, compliance and operational ownership. ### Finding A3: Access-Control Envelope Versioning and Query API Diagnosed in Honest Take but Treated as a Bag of Policy Fields in the Persistence Layer

- **Fix:** Specify the access-control envelope as a versioned, queryable artifact in the persistence schema and the architecture pattern.

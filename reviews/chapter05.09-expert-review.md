## Overall Assessment

This is the ninth recipe in Chapter 5 and the chapter's third Complex-tier recipe (four-star complexity). It introduces the recipe-specific concepts that distinguish it from the prior entity-resolution recipes (5.1 patient, 5.2 provider, 5.3 address, 5.4 eligibility, 5.5 cross-facility, 5.6 claims-clinical, 5.7 longitudinal-name-change, 5.8 privacy-preserving): the matcher operates as a federation participant against a federation of federations rather than against a single central index; the federation has no central authority that can dictate technical standards or governance posture; the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement plus the QHIN-Technical-Framework plus the Standard Operating Procedures plus the participating QHIN's framework plus the participating organizations' agreements plus the patient consent posture plus the jurisdictional overlay rules plus the use-case-specific authorization are seven concurrent governance layers that the matcher operates under simultaneously; the trust framework is operational rather than just contractual and is maintained continuously; the cross-network matching tolerance is per-use-case and calibrated separately from the internal-application tolerance; the per-record sensitivity overlay is per-jurisdiction and per-record-type with attribution at every hop; the audit-and-attribution chain captures every hop in the routing layer for forensic reconstruction; the information-blocking compliance posture interacts with the local matcher's silent-drop semantics; the cross-QHIN dispute-resolution and audit-reconstruction mechanisms are operationally non-trivial with weeks-to-months timelines; the federation's accuracy ceiling is bounded above by the data quality of the worst-quality constituent participant; the patient-experience layer is uneven across the framework and undermines patient trust; the framework's evolution outpaces the participant's adoption rate and demands a governance-evolution program with named ownership; the institutional learning curve is substantial and most institutions underestimate it. ### Finding S1: Inbound-Query Handler, Outbound-Query Formulator, Response Consolidator, Sensitivity-Overlay Applicator, Document-Retrieval Handler, QHIN-Credential Rotation, Patient-Mediated Cross-Network Query Path, Cross-Recipe EventBridge Fan-Out, and Federation-Event Producers Lack Identity-Boundary Specification With Recipe-Specific Federation-Trust-Anchor Stakes

- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Expert:** Security (PHI integrity boundary, federation-trust-anchor integrity, authorization, regulatory)
- **Location:** AWS Implementation Lambda paragraph: "Each inbound federated query is handled by a Lambda invocation that authenticates the request, validates the exchange-purpose and authorization context, and dispatches to the local matcher. Each outbound federated query is similarly handled by a Lambda that formulates the query, attaches the authorization context, and submits to the QHIN." Architecture diagram shows `EB1 -->|FanOut| C5[Recipe 5.5 cross-facility matcher]` through `C9[Internal operational systems]` and shows the QHIN-credential flow `SM1[(Secrets Manager QHIN credentials, signing keys)] --> KMS1[(AWS KMS customer-managed keys)]` plus `HSM1[(AWS CloudHSM high-assurance custody)] --> KMS1`. Step 1 `handle_inbound_patient_discovery_query()` validates the QHIN's request signature against the QHIN's public-signing-key version, validates the originating-attribution chain, and validates the exchange-purpose claim, but the per-Lambda execution-role binding, the producer-signed envelope schema for cross-recipe events, the dual-control mechanism for the QHIN-credential rotation, and the consumer-side validation for the federation-event fan-out are not specified at the architectural level. The recipe's own TODO at the IAM Permissions row explicitly enumerates the recipe-specific extensions with line-by-line specifications but the architecture text does not actually specify the boundary.

- **Problem:** The recipe specifies the TEFCA pipeline at flow-and-service granularity but is silent on the identity-boundary policy that controls who can invoke each path and what proves the caller is authorized to act on a particular query, attribution chain, exchange purpose, or QHIN-credential operation. The chapter-wide pattern from 5.1 through 5.8 has converged on a structured identity-boundary specification; Recipe 5.9 inherits the concern with eight concrete attack surfaces, several of which are uniquely sharp here:

  1. **The inbound-query-handler Lambda accepts QHIN-signed request envelopes carrying the originating-attribution chain, the exchange-purpose claim, and the demographic-feature payload.** The Step 1 pseudocode validates the QHIN's request signature against the QHIN's public-signing-key version (with the previous version included during rotation), validates the attribution chain against the participant-authorized-QHINs list, and validates the exchange-purpose claim. But the per-Lambda execution-role binding, the producer-signed envelope schema, and the rejection semantics for misconfigured QHIN-credential rotations are not specified at the architectural level. A misconfigured rotation (the participant's QHIN-key store falls behind the QHIN's actual rotation cadence; the previous-version inclusion window expires before the QHIN's actual cutover; the participant accepts a request signed under a key that has been deprecated by the QHIN's framework) silently produces wrong-record disclosures with audit-trail attribution to a QHIN that is not the actual originator.

  2. **The outbound-query-formulator Lambda formulates a request envelope signed under the participant's signing credential.** The Step 4 pseudocode signs the query under the participant's signing credential loaded from Secrets Manager. The signing credential's per-rotation Secrets Manager secret is named in the recipe text, but the architecture does not specify the producer-signed envelope's schema, the per-Lambda execution-role binding, the rotation cadence enforcement, or the audit-attribution discipline for the credential-version active at signing time. A forged outbound query (an attacker who has compromised the outbound-query-formulator Lambda's execution role and can sign queries under the participant's credential) silently produces queries that the federation routes under the participant's identity to participants whose responses will be returned to the attacker's downstream consumer.

  3. **The response-consolidator Lambda validates each responder's signature against the responder's known public key.** The Step 5 pseudocode validates the responder's signature against the responder's public-signing-key version (with the previous version included during rotation) and validates the per-response attribution chain. But the architecture does not specify how the responder's public-signing-key is provisioned at the participant, how rotation events from the QHIN's framework are propagated to the participant, or how the rejection semantics for misconfigured rotations interact with the response-window expiration. A misconfigured rotation (the participant's responder-key store falls behind the federation's actual rotation cadence; the responder's signature is rejected during the cutover; the response-window expires before the participant can re-validate against the rotated key) silently drops valid responses, which the user perceives as a partial-result presentation that the user cannot disambiguate.

  4. **The sensitivity-overlay-applicator Lambda accepts the candidate set and the authorization context and produces a filtered candidate set.** The Step 3 pseudocode applies the per-record-type sensitivity overlay (42 CFR Part 2, post-Dobbs reproductive-health-care, gender-affirming-care, additional overlays) and the per-jurisdiction overlay rules, with audit logging on every suppression event. But the architecture does not specify the per-Lambda execution-role binding, the producer-signed envelope for the overlay-rule configuration, or the consumer-side validation that the overlay-rule version active at filtering time matches the cycle's pinned version. A forged overlay-rule update (an attacker-controlled `overlay_rule_version` claim that bypasses the regulatory-monitoring function) silently downgrades the suppression posture, producing disclosures that the applicable jurisdictional rule would have suppressed.

  5. **The QHIN-credential rotation is the most consequential single event in the entire pipeline.** The QHIN-credential is the federation-trust-anchor; if the credential is compromised, the participant's federation participation is operationally suspended (the QHIN can revoke the credential under the framework's governance) or, worse, the credential is silently substituted with a credential the attacker controls and the participant's queries are signed under the attacker's identity for the period before detection. - **Seven-stage pipeline shape is correct.** Inbound-query handling with authorization context attachment, local matching under cross-network tolerance, inbound-response preparation with sensitivity-overlay application, outbound-query handling with exchange-purpose mapping and credential-signed envelope, outbound-response consolidation with per-responder signature validation and demographic-feature normalization across responders, document query and retrieval through the QHIN federation, audit / governance / dispute as cross-cutting concerns operating continuously maps cleanly to the operational reality.

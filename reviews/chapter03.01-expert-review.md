## Overall Assessment

**Verdict: PASS**

This is a strong opening recipe for the Anomaly Detection chapter. The Problem section is the densest payer-operations writing in the book so far: a claims operations supervisor with twenty-two examiners processing 180,000 claims per month, an SIU report showing 1.5-3% of paid claims as duplicates, four concrete operational-noise patterns (orthopedic 837 retransmission after a missing 277, hospital with two tax IDs, lab tech re-running a hemolyzed CBC, SNF cross-week date overlaps) that each map to a real billing scenario any payer ops leader would recognize, and the framing that "is a duplicate" hides enormous structural variation. The three-definitions-of-duplicate breakdown (exact, semantic, overlapping/adjustment) is the right teaching anchor and recurs throughout the recipe. The three-layer architecture (deterministic blocking → similarity scoring → decision and routing with feedback loop) is the architecturally correct factoring; the prose explicitly names blocking as the secret sauce, and "Honest Take" reinforces it ("a sloppy blocker that misses 20% of true duplicate pairs puts a 20% ceiling on your recall that no scorer can recover"). The fuzzy-matching section is unusually disciplined: it walks through why pure edit distance fails ("John Smith vs Jon Smyth" and `C-2026-0487291` vs `C-2026-0487219` get the same score but mean different things), then prescribes field-specific comparison functions (edit distance for system IDs, phonetic algorithms for names, day-distance for DOS, code-family lookup for CPT, relative-difference for amounts, Jaccard for sets). The label-problem subsection (selection bias, decision drift, adversarial dynamics) is the kind of hard-won-experience teaching the cookbook's voice is built on. The "Honest Take" lands the right final posture: blocking matters more than the scorer, the rule-based scorer is much harder to beat than expected, the feedback loop is what makes the system durably good, the review-queue UI determines whether examiners use the system, start with exact-match before the ML pipeline, don't confuse "detected" with "recovered."

Style hygiene is clean. Zero em dashes (direct U+2014 character check: zero matches). En dashes appear seventeen times, all in numeric ranges (cost figures, performance benchmark percentages, implementation-time tiers), consistent with Chapter 1's published precedent of three en dashes used the same way. No marketing language ("leverage," "seamless," "unlock," "transform," "empower," "revolutionize" all absent). The 70/30 vendor balance is clean: the conceptual sections (Problem, Technology, General Architecture Pattern) are vendor-neutral and a reader on GCP or Azure could substitute their cloud's primitives without rewriting the teaching; AWS service names enter in the AWS Implementation section and stay there. HTML-comment TODOs (4 total) are all forward-placeholder for cross-reference verification or industry-source citation, which is the better-than-bracket-TODO posture Chapter 2 settled into. Real, verified URLs only; no fake aws-samples repo links (the recipe is appropriately honest with HTML comments noting that a direct duplicate-claim-detection sample repo has not been confirmed).

The most consequential finding is a domain-accuracy error in the blocking-hash construction. Step 1's pseudocode uses `normalized.billing_npi[:0..4]` with the comment `// first 4 NPI digits = org-level block`. NPIs do not have hierarchical structure by organization: the National Plan and Provider Enumeration System (NPPES) assigns NPIs sequentially within the Type 1 (individual, leading 1) and Type 2 (organization, leading 2) namespaces, and the first four digits encode roughly when the NPI was issued, not which organization it belongs to. Two practices in the same hospital system can have NPIs whose first four digits differ entirely if they were enrolled in different years; conversely, two unrelated providers can share a four-digit prefix purely by issuance proximity. The blocking key as written therefore does not achieve its stated goal of catching multi-NPI organizations; it effectively reduces to "patient + arbitrary NPI prefix + month," which adds noise rather than precision. The recipe's prose acknowledges the right pattern elsewhere ("A provider-hierarchy lookup (which NPIs belong to the same tax ID) cleans this up" in Where it Struggles, and the same idea in Variations and Extensions), but the pseudocode contradicts it. This is a HIGH finding because it appears in the canonical pseudocode the reader is meant to copy, and because the pseudocode is the artifact a code-reviewer or a downstream Python companion will lift verbatim.

Several MEDIUM findings cluster on architectural completeness: trigger idempotency is not modeled at the parser-Lambda or detector-Lambda level (S3 ObjectCreated and downstream invocations are at-least-once, so a redelivered event produces duplicate suspension records and duplicate review-queue items, which is recurring across Chapter 2 reviews and applies here in a different specific form); the OpenSearch service appears in the Architecture Diagram, the Why-These-Services prose, and the Ingredients table but is not exercised anywhere in the pseudocode walkthrough (the per-field scorer does Levenshtein inline, leaving a reader to wonder when OpenSearch actually runs); SageMaker similarly appears in the diagram as an inline scoring endpoint but the walkthrough's Step 3 hardcodes the rule-based scorer with no demonstration of when or how the SageMaker call replaces it; no DLQ or poison-message handling is shown for the parser Lambda, despite the recipe's own "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" calling out 837 parsing edge cases as inevitable.

LOW findings are editorial: hot-partition risk on the `blocking_hash` partition key for high-frequency patient/provider/month combinations (chronic-care recipients, dialysis, long inpatient stays); CloudWatch monitoring (PutMetricData) interface endpoint not distinguished from CloudWatch Logs in the VPC row; modifier ordering treated as set-equivalent for hashing without acknowledging that some modifier orderings carry billing meaning; sample output uses a future date (2026-05-12) that will age awkwardly; HTML-comment TODO markers for industry-figure verification.

Priority breakdown: 0 CRITICAL, 1 HIGH, 4 MEDIUM, 5 LOW.

The risk profile is much narrower than Chapter 2's CDS recipes. There is no LLM in the core path, no clinical-recommendation surface, no FDA medical-device exposure. The downstream cost of a detector mistake is provider irritation (false-positive denials) or recoverable financial loss (false-negative leakage), not patient harm. The Chapter 2 "validation-retry safety bypass" pattern that has dominated those expert reviews does not apply here. The HIGH finding's stakes are domain accuracy and design correctness, not safety.

#### Finding S1: Reasoning-Text Field in Examiner Verdicts is PHI-Adjacent and Not Bound to a Minimization Discipline

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Security
- **Location:** Step 5 `on_examiner_verdict`, the `label.reasoning_text` field: `reasoning_text: event.reasoning_text, // free text; treat as PHI-adjacent`. Also Step 5's S3 archive write (`labels-parquet`) and DynamoDB write (`claim-labels`).
- **Problem:** The pseudocode comment correctly flags `reasoning_text` as "PHI-adjacent" but takes no protective action. Examiners writing free-text justifications in a duplicate-detection workflow routinely reference patient identifiers ("MRN 123456 was billed twice"), provider details ("Dr. Smith's office submitted under both NPIs after the merger"), and clinical context ("CBC was hemolyzed, the second draw is the one that ran"). Free text from a domain expert who has the patient record open in another tab is a high-yield PHI source. The label store is long-lived (it feeds retraining for the life of the system, with a 10-year claims retention horizon), and the field flows verbatim through DynamoDB to S3 (Parquet) to SageMaker training jobs, where it becomes a feature input to whatever scoring model gets fit. PHI minimization on free text inside the BAA boundary is the right minimum-necessary discipline; the recipe acknowledges the risk in the comment but does not implement the minimization.
- **Fix:** Add a normalization step to `on_examiner_verdict` before write that scrubs the most common identifier patterns from `reasoning_text`. A reasonable starting set (regex-based, not LLM-based, for cost and determinism reasons): MRN-shaped strings, NPI-shaped strings (10-digit numbers in identifier contexts), names following common honorifics (`Dr. \w+`, `Mr./Mrs./Ms. \w+`), DOB patterns, phone numbers. Pair with a controlled-vocabulary `reasoning_code` field (which the recipe already has) so the structured code carries the operational signal and the free text is supplementary. Add a "PHI minimization on examiner free-text" bullet to "Why This Isn't Production-Ready," and recommend AWS Comprehend Medical PHI detection as the production-grade option for the de-identification step. Same minimum-necessary-inside-the-BAA pattern that has now appeared as a recurring finding across Chapter 2 (S1 in Recipes 2.7, 2.8, 2.9, 2.10) but with a different surface: free-text human input rather than serialized prompt context.

#### Finding S2: Auto-Suspension Decision Audit Trail Not Bound to a Tamper-Evident Store

- **Severity:** LOW
- **Expert:** Security / Compliance
- **Location:** Step 4 `route_claim`, the `write_suspension_record` call inside the `IF top_match.score >= HIGH_THRESHOLD` branch.
- **Problem:** The auto-suspension record is written to a regular DynamoDB table (per the architecture, `claim-labels` or a sibling table). DynamoDB items are mutable: the table owner can update or delete the item, and the change is reversible only via point-in-time recovery within the PITR retention window (35 days). For an action that produces a provider denial and is the source of truth for any downstream provider grievance, regulatory inquiry, or SIU investigation, the auto-suspension decision record is a candidate for stronger immutability than DynamoDB's default offering. Two production patterns are common: (a) write the decision to S3 with Object Lock in COMPLIANCE mode (the recipe already specifies Object Lock COMPLIANCE on the raw-837 bucket; extending to a decisions bucket is consistent), or (b) write to QLDB (Amazon Quantum Ledger Database) for cryptographic verification of decision history. The recipe's CloudTrail data-events discipline addresses the read trail but not the write-immutability concern.
- **Fix:** Add to the Encryption row or to a new Auditability row in Prerequisites: "Auto-suspension decision records are the source of truth for provider denials and are subject to provider grievance, SIU investigation, and regulatory inquiry. Write the decision record to S3 with Object Lock in COMPLIANCE mode (the same retention pattern as raw-837), or to Amazon QLDB if cryptographic verification of decision history is required." Optionally add a one-line mention in "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" under PHI in the Review Queue and Labels.

#### Finding S3: Training Pipeline Has No Explicit De-Identification Step Before Feature Engineering

- **Severity:** LOW
- **Expert:** Security / Compliance
- **Location:** Step 5 `retrain_weekly`, the Athena query that joins `claim_labels` to `normalized_claims` for both incoming and matched claims, returning the full claim records into the training DataFrame.
- **Problem:** The training query pulls the full normalized claim records (`incoming.*`, `matched.*`) into the SageMaker training DataFrame. Those records include patient_id, billing_npi, rendering_npi, and other identifiers that the trained model can encode (intentionally or as a side effect of feature engineering). A model that learns "patient X is frequently associated with duplicate claims" or "provider org Y has a higher duplicate-rate distribution than the population" is leaking identifying patterns into the model artifact. The SageMaker model artifact is then deployed to an endpoint, where its weights become an indirect PHI exposure if the artifact is exported, copied to a non-BAA environment for analysis, or shared with a vendor for evaluation. The right production discipline is to de-identify or hash high-cardinality identifiers before training, retain only the per-field similarity components and structural features as model inputs, and explicitly reason about whether the model artifact is itself a PHI asset. The recipe's prose argues for the right ML hygiene elsewhere ("the rule-based scorer runs inline... once you graduate to a logistic regression or gradient-boosted model"), but does not call out the de-identification discipline at the training-data boundary.
- **Fix:** Add a "Training data de-identification" bullet to "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" or to the retrain step itself. The discipline: drop raw patient_id and provider NPIs from the training DataFrame, retain only the per-field similarity components (the `components_at_decision` field already has them), and treat the trained model artifact as PHI-classified storage with the same KMS and retention discipline as the label store.

### Architecture Expert Review

#### What's Done Well

- The three-layer architecture (deterministic blocking → similarity scoring → decision and routing with feedback loop) is the correct factoring for this problem class. The recipe's claim that "almost every serious duplicate detection system ends up with a three-layer architecture, whether the team designed it that way or discovered it by accident" is true and is the right anchor for the rest of the design.
- The decision to compute both a content_hash (exact-duplicate fast path) and a blocking_hash (candidate-set lookup) at parse time is the right pattern. Exact duplicates get caught at zero scoring cost; the blocking hash narrows the comparison universe before the scorer runs. The architecture's exact-duplicate GSI on content_hash with the comment "this is effectively free duplicate detection for the easy cases" is correct and is the most cost-leveraged piece of the pipeline.
- The pluggable scoring interface ("the scoring function is pluggable: the same pipeline can call into a rule-based scorer, a logistic regression, or a gradient-boosted model. Start simple; replace with learned models as labeled data accumulates") is the right teaching move. The Honest Take reinforces it ("don't skip the rules phase to rush to a learned model. You won't have labels yet anyway"), which is publication-ready voice on a point most teams get wrong.
- The two-DynamoDB-tables decision (`claim-history` for the detection path with point lookups by blocking hash; `claim-labels` for the feedback path) plus the DynamoDB Export to S3 for retraining is the architecturally correct factoring. Detection-path access patterns (point lookups) and retraining-path access patterns (bulk scans for training data) are different, and the recipe correctly separates them with the comment "DynamoDB is poor at scans; S3 + Parquet + Athena is purpose-built for them."
- The threshold-and-routing structure (HIGH_THRESHOLD → auto-suspend, LOW_THRESHOLD → auto-accept, mid-band → review queue) with the explicit caveat that the thresholds are "placeholders, not recommendations" and "should be tuned against your actual label distribution" is honest. The two-threshold design is the correct way to budget review-queue capacity independently of model choice.
- The feedback loop is specified end-to-end: examiner verdict → EventBridge → label-writer Lambda → DynamoDB + S3 → weekly SageMaker retraining job → endpoint update. The decision to gate model promotion behind a "challenger beats incumbent on held-out AUC by MIN_IMPROVEMENT" check is the correct guard against retrain-driven regressions.
- The "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" section is dense and architecturally substantive: 837 parsing edge cases, blocking-strategy measurement (with specific metrics: candidate-set size distribution p99 < 100, recall on known duplicates, block-skew), threshold tuning as ongoing not one-time, model monitoring and drift, label quality vs. quantity, retrospective sweep capability, provider communication, PHI in review queue and labels, fairness monitoring. Nine substantive bullets each tied to a real operational concern.
- Cost estimate is defensible: ~$3 Lambda + ~$20 DynamoDB + ~$75-200/month OpenSearch + ~$50-150/month SageMaker + storage = "low hundreds to low thousands per month for a 1M-claims-per-month payer," with the right framing that recovered duplicate value (1-3% of claim spend) makes the ROI strongly positive.
- Variations and Extensions covers the right adjacent patterns at the right depth: real-time at the 837 edge, embedding-based similarity on unstructured fields, graph-based detection of coordinated duplicates (Neptune), cross-payer privacy-preserving linkage (Bloom filter PPRL), LLM-assisted review triage. Each is one or two paragraphs with enough detail to scope but not so much that the recipe loses focus.
- Implementation-time tiers (4-6 weeks Basic, 4-6 months Production-ready, 6-12 months beyond for Variations) are realistic and resist the optimism-bias most ML estimates fall into.

#### Finding A1: Blocking Hash Treats NPI Prefix as Hierarchical, Which it is Not

- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Expert:** Architecture / Domain Accuracy
- **Location:** Step 1 `parse_and_normalize`, the blocking_hash construction (line 333):
  ```
  blocking_hash = sha256(
      normalized.patient_id + "|" +
      normalized.billing_npi[:0..4] + "|" +   // first 4 NPI digits = org-level block
      first_7_chars(normalized.date_of_service)   // YYYY-MM = same month
  )
  ```
- **Problem:** The comment claims the first four NPI digits act as an "org-level block." NPIs are issued by the National Plan and Provider Enumeration System (NPPES) sequentially within the Type 1 (individual provider, leading digit 1) and Type 2 (organization, leading digit 2) namespaces. The first four digits encode roughly when the NPI was issued, not which organization the provider belongs to. Two providers in the same hospital system can have NPIs whose first four digits differ entirely if they enrolled in different years; conversely, two unrelated providers can share a four-digit prefix purely because they were enrolled in the same NPPES batch. The blocking key as written therefore does not catch the multi-NPI-organization case it is meant to catch (the recipe explicitly motivates this exact case in "Where it Struggles": "Multi-tenant providers... that submit under multiple NPIs (billing NPI for the organization, rendering NPI for the individual clinician) can look like duplicates when the NPIs shift between submissions").

  The functional consequence of the bug is that the blocking key reduces to "patient + arbitrary NPI prefix + month": claims from the same exact NPI fall in the same block (correctly, because all 10 digits match including the first 4), and claims from "the same org but different NPI" land in unrelated blocks (incorrectly). The stated motivation ("first 4 NPI digits = org-level block") is not achieved.

  The recipe's prose elsewhere correctly identifies the right pattern. "Where it Struggles" says: "A provider-hierarchy lookup (which NPIs belong to the same tax ID) cleans this up." Variations and Extensions does not name the mechanism but the implication is clear. The Related Recipes section forward-references "Recipe 5.1 (Provider Identity Resolution): The 'which NPIs belong to the same billing organization?' problem this recipe mentions. A dedicated entity-resolution pipeline for provider hierarchies feeds cleaner organization IDs back into the blocking function, which improves both recall and precision." The pseudocode is the artifact that contradicts the prose.

  This is a HIGH finding because (a) the pseudocode is what readers copy, (b) the Python companion (per the existing code review at `reviews/chapter03.01-code-review.md`) implements the same pattern, and (c) the design's stated goal (multi-NPI-organization detection) is not achieved by the design as written. The risk is silent: a deployment built from this pseudocode will run, will catch exact-NPI duplicates fine, and will silently miss the multi-NPI cases the design claims to catch. Detection is the point.
- **Fix:** Three options, in increasing order of completeness:
  1. **Drop the NPI prefix from the blocking key entirely.** Use `patient_id + YYYY-MM` as the blocking hash. Rely on per-field NPI similarity in scoring to handle within-block NPI variation. Replace the comment with: "Blocking on patient + month; NPI variation across submissions is handled by per-field NPI similarity in the scorer, and multi-NPI-organization cases require the provider-hierarchy lookup described in Recipe 5.1." This is the smallest change and is functionally equivalent to what the existing key produces.
  2. **Block on tax_id (or a derived organization_id) + patient + YYYY-MM**, where tax_id comes from a provider-hierarchy lookup keyed on billing_npi. This achieves the stated multi-NPI-organization goal. Document the lookup as a precondition, with a forward reference to Recipe 5.1 (Provider Identity Resolution) for the lookup's construction. The pseudocode would call `tax_id = provider_hierarchy.lookup(normalized.billing_npi)` before constructing the blocking_hash.
  3. **Multi-blocking with a union of candidate sets**, querying the claim-history table with several blocking keys (patient + tax_id + month, patient + billing_npi + month, patient + month) and unioning results before scoring. This is the most thorough and is the right long-term posture for high-recall blocking; the recipe's "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" already mentions multi-blocking as the next-level technique.

  Option 1 is the smallest editorial change and unblocks the recipe immediately. Option 2 is the cleanest pedagogical posture because it ties the blocking design to the dedicated provider-identity-resolution recipe (5.1) the cookbook plans to publish. Either fix should be paired with a corresponding update to the Python companion to preserve pseudocode-to-Python parity.

#### Finding A2: Trigger Idempotency Not Modeled at the Parser or Detector Lambda

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture
- **Location:** Step 1 `parse_and_normalize` (S3 ObjectCreated → parser Lambda); Step 2 `find_candidates` (parser → detector); Step 4 `route_claim` (detector → SQS / DynamoDB writes).
- **Problem:** The architecture is event-driven across two Lambda hops (S3 ObjectCreated → parser, parser-output → detector). S3 ObjectCreated events are at-least-once delivery; Lambda asynchronous invocations are at-least-once with default retry semantics. If S3 redelivers an event (the parser's first attempt timed out at the SDK boundary, or the asynchronous Lambda invocation queue retried), the parser runs twice on the same 837 file. The current pseudocode has no idempotency guard: each invocation generates fresh content/blocking hashes from the same input data (which collide correctly, so the DynamoDB `PutItem` is idempotent at the claim level by `claim_id`), but the downstream detector is then triggered twice for the same incoming claim, which produces two suspension records (Step 4's `write_suspension_record` is unconditional), two SQS review-queue messages, and two examiner-queue entries.

  Concrete consequences: an examiner sees the same claim pair surfaced twice in their queue; the auto-suspension audit trail has two records for the same decision; CloudWatch metrics double-count auto-suspension decisions; the label store gets two label rows when the examiner resolves the (one) underlying review. The duplicate-detection pipeline produces a duplicate of itself.

  This is the same trigger-idempotency pattern flagged across Recipes 2.4 through 2.10 expert reviews, surfacing here in a different form (S3 ObjectCreated and asynchronous Lambda fan-out rather than EventBridge). The fix template is the same: derive a deterministic invocation key, enforce single-write at the orchestration layer.
- **Fix:** Two-part fix. First, in Step 1, make the parser idempotent at the S3-event level. The `claim_id` and `content_hash` are deterministic from the input; the existing `DynamoDB.PutItem` on `claim-history` should use a conditional write (`attribute_not_exists(claim_id)`) so a duplicate event produces a `ConditionalCheckFailedException` rather than a no-op overwrite, and the parser logs the duplicate-event signal. Second, in Step 4, before `write_suspension_record`, do a conditional write on a `decisions` table keyed on `(incoming_claim_id, matched_claim_id, model_version)`: a successful conditional write proceeds with the SQS send; a failed conditional write means the decision was already made and skips the SQS send. Add a "Trigger idempotency" bullet to "Why This Isn't Production-Ready." This recurring pattern is now a chapter-wide candidate for a shared appendix; if Chapter 3's preface or a cookbook-wide appendix consolidates the trigger-idempotency pattern (S3 events, EventBridge, Kinesis, SQS, Step Functions deterministic execution names), each subsequent recipe can reference it.

#### Finding A3: OpenSearch is in the Architecture but Not in the Walkthrough

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture / Pedagogy
- **Location:** Architecture Diagram (`G[(Amazon OpenSearch\nclaim-search)]` and the edge `E -->|Fuzzy field queries| G`); Why-These-Services paragraph on Amazon OpenSearch Service ("Edit-distance and phonetic matching on patient names, claim numbers, and provider names benefit from a search engine that's built for them"); Ingredients table; Step 3 `score_pair` pseudocode (no OpenSearch call); Step 2 `find_candidates` (no OpenSearch call).
- **Problem:** OpenSearch appears prominently in three places (diagram, prose, ingredients) and is described as the home of fuzzy field matching. The pseudocode walkthrough never calls OpenSearch. Step 3's `_field_similarity` does Levenshtein inline in Lambda; Step 2's `find_candidates` does the blocking lookup against DynamoDB only. A reader following the walkthrough has no idea when OpenSearch actually runs. The recipe says "Edit-distance and phonetic matching on patient names, claim numbers, and provider names benefit from a search engine that's built for them. OpenSearch supports edit-distance queries, phonetic tokenizers, and k-nearest-neighbor vector search in a single index. For a mid-size payer, it's cheaper and simpler to manage than running a separate similarity microservice." But the walkthrough does inline Levenshtein, not OpenSearch fuzzy queries; and the per-field similarity for `patient_id` and `billing_npi` is straight Levenshtein (not phonetic, not patient-name fuzzy). The phonetic-matching motivation in the prose is for patient names, but the Step 3 weight set has no patient-name field at all (the per-field weights are: patient_id, billing_npi, rendering_npi, date_of_service, cpt_code, modifiers, billed_amount, diagnosis_codes, place_of_service).

  This produces two pedagogical problems: (a) a reader implementing the recipe can omit OpenSearch entirely and the walkthrough still works (it's the design Python companion takes, per the code review), which makes the OpenSearch recommendation feel optional and decorative; (b) a reader who does spin up OpenSearch has no concrete pattern for when to call it from the detector Lambda, what fields to index, or how the OpenSearch results compose with the in-Lambda similarity score.
- **Fix:** Two reasonable resolutions:
  1. **Demote OpenSearch in the architecture.** Move it from the Architecture Diagram and the core Why-These-Services paragraph into Variations and Extensions ("If you have a patient-name or provider-name field with significant fuzzy-match volume, OpenSearch's phonetic tokenizers and edit-distance queries are the right backend for that field's similarity computation; query OpenSearch from the detector Lambda for the name fields and use the result as an additional scorer feature"). Keep the ingredients table entry but mark it as optional. This aligns the architecture with what the walkthrough actually does.
  2. **Promote OpenSearch into the walkthrough.** Add an explicit Step 2.5 (or expand Step 3) that demonstrates an OpenSearch call: "After the DynamoDB blocking lookup returns the candidate set, query OpenSearch for any candidate within edit-distance N or phonetic-match on the patient-name field" with concrete pseudocode. Add a `patient_name_similarity` field to the FIELD_WEIGHTS map. This is more architecturally complete but requires a non-trivial pseudocode addition and a corresponding Python companion update.

  Option 1 is the smaller editorial change and is the more honest posture for the rule-based-scorer recipe the walkthrough actually teaches. Option 2 is the cleaner long-term direction. Either choice should match what the Python companion does, which currently aligns with Option 1.

#### Finding A4: SageMaker Endpoint in Architecture Diagram Implies a Production Inference Path the Walkthrough Does Not Demonstrate

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture / Pedagogy
- **Location:** Architecture Diagram (`H[SageMaker Endpoint\nscoring-model]` with the edge `E -->|Score candidate pairs| H`); Step 3 pseudocode `score_pair` (rule-based scorer inline, no SageMaker call); Why-These-Services paragraph on Amazon SageMaker.
- **Problem:** Similar in shape to A3. The diagram shows the detector calling a SageMaker endpoint to score candidate pairs; Step 3's pseudocode hardcodes the rule-based scorer (`FUNCTION score_pair(incoming, candidate): ... FOR each field_name, weight in FIELD_WEIGHTS: ...`) with no SageMaker invocation. The Why-These-Services prose says "The rule-based scorer runs inline in Lambda. #### Finding A5: No DLQ or Poison-Message Handling for the 837 Parser

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture
- **Location:** Architecture Diagram (`A[837 EDI Files...] -->|PUT| B[S3 Bucket\nraw-837/]`; `B -->|ObjectCreated Event| C[AWS Lambda\n837-parser]`); Prerequisites table.
- **Problem:** The architecture has no Dead Letter Queue (DLQ) or destination-on-failure configured for the parser Lambda. The recipe's own "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" calls out 837 parsing edge cases as inevitable: "Your parser will hit edge cases in the first week of production that never appeared in testing." When that happens, Lambda's default async retry behavior (two retries over six hours, then drop) will silently lose the malformed 837 file's processing record. The raw 837 stays in S3 (per the system-of-record discipline), but the operational state of "this transaction was received but failed to parse" is not captured anywhere outside CloudWatch Logs for the Lambda invocation. A trading partner asking "did you get our submission from May 12?" cannot be answered from the claim-history table because parsing failed before write; the answer requires log-trawling.

  The right pattern: configure the parser Lambda's `OnFailure` destination to an SQS queue (or SNS topic) so failed parses become first-class operational artifacts. The poison-message queue is monitored, alerted on, and triaged by the on-call team; messages can be replayed after the parser is updated.
- **Fix:** Add to the Architecture Diagram a `parser-dlq` SQS queue receiving the parser Lambda's `OnFailure` destination; add a one-line note in the Why-These-Services or Prerequisites section: "Configure each Lambda's `OnFailure` destination to a dedicated SQS DLQ; raw events that exhaust retries become first-class operational artifacts, monitored with a CloudWatch alarm on queue depth. Replay after fixing the parser." Optionally add a Step 1 prefatory note: "The parser Lambda is configured with `MaximumRetryAttempts=2` and `OnFailure` destination set to the parser-dlq; malformed 837 files surface as DLQ messages with the original event payload." This same pattern should apply to the detector Lambda and the label-writer Lambda (each gets its own DLQ, scoped to its own failure modes).

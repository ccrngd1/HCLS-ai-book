## Overall Assessment

**Verdict: PASS**

This is the second recipe in the chapter that lands at the "Complex" complexity tier with full production governance scope, and the writing meets the moment. The Mr. Alvarez vignette (71 years old, ten days post-admit for heart failure exacerbation, EF 30%, two days step-down then six on cardiology floor, diuresed gently and optimized on guideline-directed medical therapy, taught daily weights, given a pillbox and the new med list, scheduled for cardiology follow-up at seven days, discharged Thursday afternoon, weights tracking 198 to 199 to 201 to 204 over Friday-Saturday-Sunday-Monday, sleeping on three pillows by Sunday, daughter taking him to the ED on Monday morning, readmitted with acute decompensated heart failure for IV diuretics and three more inpatient days at twelve thousand dollars and a meaningful hit to quality of life and trust) lands the operational reality of post-discharge deterioration in five paragraphs. The closing reframe ("of all the patients we discharged in the last 30 days, whose post-discharge trajectory right now is deviating from what successful recovery looks like, and where can a phone call, a home visit, or a same-day clinic slot keep them out of the ED?") is the question first-time builders miss because they conflate it with discharge-time scoring, and the recipe states the distinction plainly.

The "Why Anomaly Detection Instead of Just Predict Readmission" subsection is the recipe's biggest pedagogical contribution and is the lesson most published treatments of post-discharge programs blur. The framing that discharge-time risk scoring (LACE, LACE+, HOSPITAL, Epic, various commercial models; mostly Chapter 7 territory) and post-discharge anomaly detection are complementary rather than competing, that the former sets up monitoring intensity at the moment of discharge and the latter consumes the monitoring data and surfaces patients whose actual trajectories are diverging from expected, is exactly right and is the design lesson that distinguishes mature programs from immature ones. The "deviation from expected" framing for the model output (a patient at 200 lbs is normal if they discharged at 198, anomalous if they discharged at 175) is the operationally-correct framing.

The Operational Anatomy of a Post-Discharge Episode subsection (Day 0 discharge with teach-back often skipped; Day 1-3 highest-risk window with medication errors and missed first follow-up and transportation issues; Day 4-7 integration window with first PRO check-in and prescription pickup; Day 8-21 drift window where existing programs do less well because the highest-touch outreach is concentrated in the first week; Day 22-30 end-of-window risk often related to medication non-adherence and gradual decompensation; Day 30+ post-window observation with the CMS-construct framing of the 30-day cutoff) is the densest treatment of post-discharge timeline anywhere in the cookbook. The "the pipeline has to produce useful output at every point in this timeline, not just at the moment of discharge" closer is the operational requirement that distinguishes streaming-cadence programs from batch-cadence programs.

The Data Sources subsection (PROs through patient-facing app or SMS or IVR; RPM device data through vendor cloud aggregators including BodyTrace, A&D Medical, iHealth, Withings, Roche, Abbott; CGM as a high-cadence sub-category; wearables; EHR feeds; HIE data with the regional-coverage caveat; claims feeds for ACO/risk-sharing arrangements; pharmacy data including Surescripts and PBM feeds; SDOH data including PRAPARE, AHC HRSN, Z-codes; care management notes; caregiver-reported data) is unusually substantive. The "engagement decay" framing as a first-class signal ("a previously-engaged patient who suddenly stops checking in often warrants outreach") is the kind of detail-level teaching that distinguishes the cookbook from documentation. The "absence of data is itself the most common signal" reframe is the operationally-correct framing for the missing-data problem.

The Statistical and ML Methods subsection (patient-specific control charts including CUSUM and EWMA and Shewhart; forecasting plus residual analysis with ARIMA / exponential smoothing / state-space; multivariate change-point detection including PELT and Bayesian online change-point; recurrent neural networks; transformer-based clinical time series models including BEHRT and Med-BERT; survival analysis with DeepSurv and DeepHit; gradient-boosted trees on engineered time-window features; hybrid scoring; causal-inference-aware models) is the broadest method survey across multiple temporal resolutions in any chapter-3 recipe. The closing "reasonable progression" posture (start with patient-specific control charts on high-signal modalities, augmented by engagement-decay and discharge-time risk score; validate against care-management judgment; add gradient-boosted model once outcome labels are flowing; add LSTM or transformer only if the marginal gain justifies the operational complexity) is operations-engineer voice on the most consequential program-design decision.

The Outcome Definition subsection is unusually substantive and lands the same lesson Recipe 3.7 lands ("the choice of outcome shapes what your model learns") for a different timescale. All-cause readmission within 30 days all-facilities (the HRRP-relevant definition); condition-specific readmission within 30 days (CMS uses condition-specific definitions for HRRP penalty calculation); ED visits without admission (often clinically equivalent but doesn't trigger HRRP penalties); observation stays (technically not readmission, clinically often equivalent); intermediate clinical events (new diuretic dose, new oxygen prescription, new clinic visit); mortality as a competing risk with the survival-analysis-with-competing-risks framing (Fine-Gray, multi-state models). The closing "use a composite outcome: 30-day all-cause readmission OR ED visit OR death OR observation stay, with secondary analyses on the condition-specific HRRP-relevant subset" is the practical guidance most published treatments dance around.

The Features-That-Actually-Matter subsection has nine feature classes (discharge-time snapshot, RPM trajectory features per modality, PRO-derived features, EHR-derived features, care management interaction features, social and behavioral features, time-since-discharge features, engagement features, patient-specific baselines and deviations) and the "patient-specific baselines and deviations are the single most important feature class for separating 'this number is normal for them' from 'this number is concerning'" framing is the kind of math-meets-clinical-reasoning teaching the cookbook is built on. The Cold-Start-Is-The-Default-State subsection (tier-based monitoring; cohort-based priors; engagement-first triage with the right framing that "the absence of data is treated as a signal"; hybrid scoring with backoff) is the right operational framing.

The Calibration, Subgroup Performance, and the Equity Question subsection is unusually substantive on equity. The "the deployment design matters as much as the model design" reframe and the three named approaches (explicitly weighting intervention prioritization by social vulnerability indices to counter bias in the underlying data; subgroup-stratified thresholds; post-hoc fairness-aware allocation) is the operationally-honest framing. The Workflow-Integration-Is-The-Actual-Product subsection lands the same lesson as Recipe 3.7 with a post-discharge-specific factoring (daily care management worklist sorted by composite score with explanations visible inline; outreach scripts and decision support; escalation pathways; documentation back into the EHR; closed-loop with discharge planning).

#### Finding A1: Outcome-Event and Intervention-Event Idempotency Not Modeled at the Capture Lambdas

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture
- **Location:** Step 8 `on_outcome_event` (consumed via EventBridge from the EHR-events stream and HIE/claims feeds for readmission, ED visit, death, observation stay events); Step 8 `on_care_manager_action` (consumed via EventBridge from the care management UI back ends); Architecture Diagram (`AJ[Readmission / ED visit / death events] --> AK[AWS Lambda outcome-capture]`, `AH[Care manager / nurse / pharmacist actions] --> AI[AWS Lambda intervention-capture]`).
- **Problem:** The architecture is event-driven from the EHR, HIE, and claims feeds for outcome events (readmissions to your hospital and to other facilities, ED visits, post-discharge mortality, observation stays, program graduations) and from the care management UI for intervention events (outreach attempted, contact made, intervention delivered, escalations made). EventBridge guarantees at-least-once delivery; Lambda asynchronous invocation also retries on failure. If EventBridge redelivers an outcome event, `on_outcome_event` runs twice on the same outcome. Each run:

  1. **Searches OpenSearch for recent scores and recent interventions** within `OUTCOME_LINKAGE_WINDOW`. The OpenSearch search is idempotent in shape, but the linkage assembly produces a `label_row` whose `recent_score_ids`, `recent_intervention_ids`, and `feature_snapshots` are derived from the same query with potentially-different timestamps if the redelivery has a slightly-different `received_at`.

  2. **Writes a fresh label row to S3 with a UUID-keyed path** (`S3.PutObject(... key = f"outcomes/year={...}/month={...}/{generate_id()}.json", ...)`). Two label rows for the same outcome get picked up by the next quarterly retraining job. For a post-discharge program where positive cases (readmissions) are rare and precious labels (the base rate is typically a few percent and the labels drive the supervised classifier's class-balance), doubled labels on positive cases distort the supervised classifier's training distribution toward whichever cases happened to be redelivered.

  3. **For readmissions, transitions `state.is_active = false` and sets `state.program_end_reason = "readmission"` and `state.program_end_at = outcome_event.occurred_at`.** A redelivered readmission event with a slightly-different `occurred_at` overwrites the program-end timestamp inconsistently.

  Same recurring trigger-idempotency pattern as Recipes 2.4-2.10, 3.1, 3.2, 3.3, 3.4, 3.5, 3.6, 3.7 (thirteenth consecutive recipe). The fix template is the same.

  For Recipe 3.8 specifically the consequence is calibrated to two pathways: the supervised retraining pathway (doubled label rows on positive cases distort class balance for the gradient-boosted composite model and for any LSTM/transformer variant trained on the labeled data) and the program-state pathway (a redelivered readmission event can clobber the program-end timestamp with a value that differs by milliseconds to seconds, which propagates into incorrect `days_post_discharge` computations for any subsequent outcome events that occur in the same window).

  Same applies to `on_care_manager_action`: redelivered intervention events double-append to `state.intervention_history`, double-update `state.last_contact_at` (with potentially-different timestamps if the redelivery has a different `received_at`), and double-write to the intervention-history table. For RPM CPT code billing documentation, doubled intervention events on the same care-management interaction produce billable-activity capture errors that downstream billing teams will catch only after the documentation has been submitted.

  With this finding now flagged across thirteen consecutive recipes, the per-recipe-edit posture is producing diminishing returns. The cookbook editor should treat the trigger-idempotency appendix as the highest-leverage cookbook-wide editorial investment.
- **Fix:** Two-part fix:
  1. Derive a deterministic event key from each event's identifier and use it as a write-once guard in DynamoDB before the OpenSearch search, the S3 label write, and the state-transition update:
     ```
     # Outcome event idempotency.
     event_key = outcome_event.event_id + "|" + outcome_event.type
     try:
         DynamoDB.PutItem(
             table = "processed-outcome-events",
             item  = { event_key: event_key, processed_at: NOW() },
             condition = "attribute_not_exists(event_key)"
         )
     except ConditionalCheckFailedException:
         emit_metric("outcome_event_duplicate_dropped", 1)
         RETURN
     # ... rest of on_outcome_event ...
     ```
     The `processed-outcome-events` table can have a TTL of 90 days. Apply the same pattern to `on_care_manager_action` keyed on `action_event.event_id`.
  2. Add a "Trigger idempotency" bullet to "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" tying the discipline to the recurring chapter pattern. Strongly recommend a cookbook-wide trigger-idempotency appendix.

#### Finding A2: No DLQ or Poison-Message Handling for the Ingest, Normalize, Score, Worklist-Build, Intervention-Capture, or Outcome-Capture Lambdas

- **Severity:** MEDIUM
- **Expert:** Architecture
- **Location:** Architecture Diagram (`B[API Gateway + AWS Lambda device-ingest]`, `E[AWS Lambda ehr-event-ingest]`, `H[AWS Lambda cm-event-ingest]`, `J[AWS Lambda enrollment-handler]`, `L[AWS Lambda event-normalizer]`, `R[AWS Lambda event-driven-rescore]`, `S[AWS Lambda feature-engine]`, `AA[AWS Lambda worklist-builder]`, `AI[AWS Lambda intervention-capture]`, `AK[AWS Lambda outcome-capture]` all without `OnFailure` destinations); Prerequisites table.
- **Problem:** No Dead Letter Queue or `OnFailure` destination is configured for any Lambda in the pipeline. Specific failure modes that surface here:

  - **Device-ingest Lambda fails on a malformed RPM webhook payload.** The vendor publishes a new firmware version that adds a previously-unspecified field, the unit-conversion encounters an unhandled unit string (a vendor that ships in stones-and-pounds for UK deployments), the patient master-index resolution times out because the device-to-patient mapping cache is stale. Lambda's default async retry is two retries over six hours and then drop. The result is silently lost: no canonical event reaches the patient state store, no scoring is updated for that patient, the trajectory history is missing the data point. For a program where engagement decay is one of the strongest signals, silently-failing ingestion looks operationally identical to patient disengagement and triggers outreach for a problem that was actually a pipeline failure.

  - **Event-normalizer Lambda fails during state update or Timestream write** (DynamoDB throttle on patient-state during a thundering-herd discharge event, Timestream write timeout on a slow-storage operation, schema-validation failure on a vendor's unexpected payload). Same retry-then-drop pattern. The patient state and trajectory history go out of sync.

  - **Feature-engine Lambda fails during feature computation** (Timestream query timeout on the rolling-window aggregation, Feature Store write throttle, Bedrock throttling on the explanation generation). The patient is not scored on the affected event. For the daily-tick scoring path, a single failure can be retried at the next tick; for the event-driven re-scoring path triggered by an external admission or ED visit, the missed re-scoring is a missed prioritization opportunity that may not be caught up before the urgent intervention window closes.

  - **Worklist-builder Lambda fails during worklist construction** (DynamoDB read throttle on scoring-history during the daily run, OpenSearch indexing failure, Bedrock throttling on the per-row narrative generation). The worklist is not produced or is produced incompletely. Care managers either get no worklist or get a worklist with missing rows for some patients.

  - **Intervention-capture Lambda fails on a malformed action event** (the care-management UI emits an unexpected `action_type` value, the `worklist_id` references a worklist that has aged out of retention, the intervention free-text exceeds the DynamoDB attribute size limit). The intervention is never recorded; the worklist row stays "pending" forever; the care-manager-time-on-patient metric is never computed for that intervention; the RPM CPT code billing-activity capture is incomplete.

  - **Outcome-capture Lambda fails on a malformed outcome event** (the EHR or HIE emits an unexpected outcome-type code, the patient_id references a patient outside the program scope, the linkage fails because of an intermediate-state mismatch). The label is never written; the case never enters the labeled-outcomes archive; the supervised classifier's next retraining run misses the data point; the post-discharge program loses critical positive-class signal.

  Without DLQs, these failures appear only in CloudWatch Logs and require log-trawling to discover. For a system in the care-management decision path of post-acute-event patients, where the FDA CDS exemption posture depends on the system operating as designed and the RPM CPT code billing posture depends on the documentation being complete and accurate, silently-failing scoring is a CDS-and-billing-posture concern in addition to an operational one.
- **Fix:** Add ten SQS DLQs to the Architecture Diagram: `device-ingest-dlq`, `ehr-event-ingest-dlq`, `cm-event-ingest-dlq`, `enrollment-handler-dlq`, `event-normalizer-dlq`, `feature-engine-dlq`, `worklist-builder-dlq`, `intervention-capture-dlq`, `outcome-capture-dlq`, `event-driven-rescore-dlq`. Add a Prerequisites note: "Configure each Lambda's `OnFailure` destination to a dedicated SQS DLQ. CloudWatch alarms on DLQ depth alert the on-call program-engineering team; for the device-ingest-dlq, ehr-event-ingest-dlq, event-normalizer-dlq, and outcome-capture-dlq specifically, alarm threshold should be 1 because a single dropped event is a patient-state gap, a missed engagement signal, or a missed positive-class label. Replay events from DLQ after fixing the root cause; for events older than the post-discharge prediction window relevant to the patient's current risk tier, escalate to care-management-governance-committee review rather than auto-replay because the timing-of-detection is itself part of the program's safety case." Add a "DLQ and replay" bullet to "Why This Isn't Production-Ready" that ties the discipline to the existing disaster-recovery paragraph.

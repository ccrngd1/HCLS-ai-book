## Overall Assessment

This is the ninth recipe in Chapter 10 (Speech / Voice AI) and the chapter's third complex-tier recipe after 10.7 (ambient clinical documentation) and 10.8 (voice biomarker detection). The recipe pivots cleanly from 10.8's voice-biomarker-as-disease-state framing by inverting the clinical question: voice biomarker recipes care about acoustic features that correlate with disease state and treat the linguistic content as secondary; speech-therapy assessment cares about what the patient is producing at the phonetic, prosodic, and fluency levels, against established clinical scoring rubrics, with the impaired speech as the explicit target. The opening Maya-and-the-flipbook vignette (the six-year-old saying "wabbit" for "rabbit," "seep" for "sheep," "fum" for "thumb" while the SLP makes notes between sessions and carries the documentation backlog home) earns its position as the chapter's strongest single articulation of the SLP-as-customer-with-real-time-pressure problem and grounds the recipe in the felt-experience of a clinical workflow that is documentation-bottlenecked rather than data-poor.

The recipe correctly positions itself as the recipe in the chapter where "the workflow integration challenge is largest, where the SLP-as-customer framing matters most, and where the difference between 'useful clinical tool' and 'annoying productivity drag' is determined more by interface and workflow choices than by the underlying AI accuracy," with explicit deferrals to recipes 10.4 (medical transcription), 10.6 (telehealth speech-to-text), and 10.8 (voice biomarker detection) for the audio-capture-infrastructure overlap and to recipes 2.5 (after-visit summary) and 2.6 (clinical note summarization) for the LLM-driven summarization patterns. This composition discipline is a recipe strength and avoids the chapter-pattern repetition problem. The recipe-distinct contributions to the chapter are the disordered-speech-as-explicit-target-not-degraded-edge-case primitive, the established-assessment-instruments-anchor-clinical-credibility primitive, the SLP-in-the-loop-with-confidence-based-review-flags-as-clinically-defensible-framing primitive, the per-instrument-and-per-population-validation-as-architectural-primitive, the longitudinal-trajectory-often-beats-single-session-assessment primitive, the pediatric-and-adult-as-different-products primitive, the school-context-FERPA-COPPA-pediatric-records-retention discipline, the cross-dialect-generalization-as-equity-stakes primitive, and the deployment-context-heterogeneity-across-hospital-school-private-practice-telepractice-early-intervention-inpatient as architectural-configuration primitive.

### Finding S1: Voice-as-Biometric-Data Architectural Governance Scaffolding Underspecified Despite Explicit Prose Elevation in Multiple Places, with Recipe-Distinct Pediatric-Records and Long-Retention-Horizon Amplification

- **Severity:** HIGH
- **Expert:** Security (biometric-data regulatory compliance, voice-sample lifecycle, pediatric-records protection, FERPA-and-COPPA layering)
- **Location:**
  - Prerequisites BAA / Compliance row: "Voice samples are biometric data; biometric-data law (Illinois BIPA, Texas, Washington) applies in addition to HIPAA where the patient's jurisdiction triggers it. School deployments add FERPA considerations. Pediatric deployments add COPPA considerations for any direct-to-child interface elements."
  - Cross-Cutting Design Points: "Audio retention policy is bounded by consent and protected by encryption. Voice samples from speech-therapy assessment are biometric data."
  - Step 1C pseudocode `consent_outcome = capture_consent(...)` with `consent_type: "speech_therapy_assessment"`, `applicable_frameworks: determine_consent_frameworks(patient_context, deployment_context)`, `is_minor: patient_context.is_minor`.
  - Step 8A pseudocode `schedule_audio_deletion(audio_refs, delete_after: lookup_audio_retention(consent_id, deployment_context))`.
  - Where-it-Struggles "Pediatric data privacy and the long retention horizon" item: "A six-year-old being assessed today has data that may need to be retained until age 25 or longer depending on state pediatric-records law. The biometric voice samples are part of that long-horizon data."
  - Honest Take's twelfth trap: "explicit pediatric-records-retention policies, audit-archive infrastructure that supports the long retention floor, explicit deletion-on-request workflow that respects pediatric protections including parent-on-behalf-of-minor and patient-once-of-age requests."
  - Production-Gaps "Pediatric-specific consent infrastructure" paragraph.

- **Problem:** The recipe correctly elevates voice-as-biometric-data and the recipe-distinct pediatric-records-and-long-retention-horizon amplification in five separate places and correctly identifies BIPA, Texas, Washington, FERPA, and COPPA as applicable regulatory regimes. Despite this thorough prose elevation, the architecture pattern, the diagram, and the pseudocode treat the biometric-data governance and the pediatric-records governance as an under-specified set of references without specifying the governance scaffolding. Recipe-acute and recipe-distinct because:

  1. **Pediatric speech-therapy workflows produce many samples per patient over a multi-year therapy course, maximizing per-patient regulatory exposure under both BIPA and pediatric-records-retention statutes.** A child in ongoing speech therapy may produce hundreds of voice samples over years of treatment (a typical pediatric phonological-disorder treatment course spans 6-24 months with weekly or biweekly sessions). Each sample is a biometric-identifier collection event under BIPA, CUBI, and Washington's biometric-data law (statutory damages of $1,000 to $5,000 per violation) AND a pediatric medical-records collection event under state pediatric-records-retention statutes (which extend retention to age-of-majority-plus-multiple-years; in some states, age 25+ for a six-year-old patient today). The recipe-distinct interaction between BIPA-style biometric-records retention (which generally requires deletion when the disclosed purpose ends) and pediatric-medical-records retention (which generally requires extended retention beyond age of majority) creates a tension the architecture does not resolve.

  2. **The disclosure-accounting log is not specified.** BIPA generally requires institutions that collect biometric identifiers to maintain a disclosure-accounting log (the collection event, the purpose, the retention period, each subsequent use) in writing. The recipe captures `consent_id` and `consent_outcome.granted` at Step 1C but does not specify the per-use disclosure-accounting log entry that each subsequent assessment scoring, each SLP review access, each post-deployment surveillance use, each parent-summary generation, each EHR or SIS write-back, and each potential third-party disclosure should produce.

  3. **The right-to-deletion workflow is not architecturally specified, and is recipe-distinctly complicated by the parent-on-behalf-of-minor-and-patient-once-of-age handoff.** The Honest Take's twelfth trap explicitly raises the parent-on-behalf-of-minor-and-patient-once-of-age handoff but the architecture does not specify the deletion-propagation discipline. A parent's request for biometric-data deletion under BIPA on behalf of a minor must propagate to: the audio sample (typically already deleted under the brief-retention policy), the feature vectors derived from the audio, the per-item scores derived from the feature vectors, the longitudinal trajectory entries that incorporate those scores, the audit-archive disclosure-accounting log entries, and the goal-attainment-tracking data. A patient (now an adult) requesting deletion of their pediatric speech-therapy records under the per-state pediatric-records-protection statute, BIPA, or other applicable framework requires the same propagation but with the parent-on-behalf authority replaced with the patient-on-own-behalf authority. The architecture supports the audio deletion (Step 8A `schedule_audio_deletion(...)`) but does not architect the feature-vector deletion, the score deletion, the trajectory-entry deletion, the goal-attainment deletion, the disclosure-accounting log entry, or the parent-on-behalf-to-patient-on-own-behalf authority handoff.

  4. **The feature-vector biometric classification is not specified.** The recipe does not address whether the feature vectors derived from voice (the per-phoneme acoustic boundaries, the per-phoneme classification, the prosodic features, the voice-quality features, the linguistic features from connected-speech transcripts) are themselves biometric data. The S3 feature-vector bucket uses a separate KMS key from the audio bucket (good, blast-radius containment) but the bucket's classification, access controls, retention default, and deletion-workflow integration are implicit.

  5. **Per-jurisdiction key-management is not architecturally elevated.** The recipe does not specify per-jurisdiction key isolation. Per-jurisdiction key isolation typically requires separate KMS keys per jurisdiction (so a deletion request for a BIPA-jurisdiction patient or a school-deployment-jurisdiction patient under FERPA can cryptographically erase the data even if the lifecycle-policy deletion has not yet completed) and per-jurisdiction S3 prefixes / DynamoDB partition keys to support the cryptographic isolation.

  6. **Voice-cloning and synthetic-voice-detection threat model is not architected.** A voice sample, once exfiltrated, can be used to clone the patient's voice via voice-cloning AI. Pediatric voice-cloning has additional threat-model dimensions because pediatric voices in longitudinal therapy archives provide a multi-year acoustic trajectory that supports more-realistic cloning than single-sample collections. The architecture should specify defense-in-depth measures: tamper-evident logging on the audio bucket access, watermarking or fingerprinting of audio samples for breach-detection, synthetic-voice-detection in the validation pipeline (so a cloned voice cannot be used to impersonate a patient in subsequent assessment submissions), and breach-response playbooks specific to biometric-data exfiltration with the recipe-distinct pediatric-voice-cloning amplification.

  7. **GDPR Article 9 special-category-data treatment for EU patients is not addressed.** The recipe correctly notes biometric-data-law applicability for U.S. jurisdictions but does not address GDPR Article 9 for EU-resident speech-therapy patients. The architecture should specify the per-region deployment model.

  8. **FERPA is referenced but not architected as a per-deployment-context configuration.** The recipe correctly notes FERPA applicability for school deployments but does not architect the FERPA-aligned consent flow, the FERPA-aligned access controls (school-employee-only access for educational records; the IEP team's narrower access scope; the parent-access-by-default pattern under FERPA), or the FERPA-and-HIPAA dual-jurisdiction handling for school-based services billed to private health insurance.

  9. **COPPA is referenced but not architected for direct-to-child interfaces.** The recipe correctly notes COPPA applicability for any direct-to-child interface elements (typical for the home-practice-app and parent-coaching variations) but does not architect the COPPA-aligned consent flow (verifiable parental consent before any direct-to-child data collection), the COPPA-aligned data-minimization defaults, or the COPPA-aligned access-controls for child-account-credentials versus parent-account-credentials.

  10. **The pediatric-records-extending-to-age-of-majority-plus-X retention is acknowledged in prose but not specified as architectural primitive.** The Where-it-Struggles "Pediatric data privacy and the long retention horizon" item acknowledges the long retention horizon but the architecture does not specify how the per-state pediatric-records-retention rule (which varies; California is age-of-majority-plus-1-year, New York is 6 years from majority, Texas is age-25, etc.) is detected, applied, or audited.

- **Fix:** Promote the biometric-data governance and the pediatric-records governance from a passing reference to an architectural primitive matching the structure recommended in Recipe 10.7 Finding S1 and Recipe 10.8 Finding S1 with recipe-distinct pediatric-records and FERPA-and-COPPA dimensions. Specifically:

  - Add a "Voice-as-Biometric-Data Governance Scaffolding with Pediatric-Records, FERPA, and COPPA Layering" subsection to the architecture pattern's Cross-Cutting Design Points:
    > "Voice samples and the feature vectors derived from them are subject to a biometric-data governance profile that complies with Illinois BIPA, Texas CUBI, Washington's biometric-data law, GDPR Article 9 for EU patients, and any other applicable jurisdiction's biometric-data statutes.
